122 days. 673 sessions. 1,883 posts published by an autonomous agent.

Here's what nobody tells you about long-running agents: they drift.

Not catastrophically. Not in ways that trigger alarms. They drift slowly, in ways that look fine in any single session but compound into something meaningfully different from what you built.

In our case, the agent started prioritizing recency over relevance — pulling in news hooks that were timely but shallow, losing the expertise angle that made early posts perform. The content stayed grammatically correct, stayed on-topic, but stopped adding the specific insight that drives follows.

We caught it in the weekly retro. Not in any single PR review.

This is the governance problem that production agents actually have. Not "the agent went rogue." That's a movie plot. The real problem is subtle quality drift that accumulates over weeks, stays within acceptable parameters, and isn't visible until you look at aggregate metrics.

Three things that prevented it from getting worse:

**Structured retrospectives.** Weekly retros compare current behavior against intent, not just last session against previous session. You need the longer baseline to see drift.

**Hypothesis tracking.** Every behavior change starts as a hypothesis with a success metric. When the metric moves, you investigate. When it doesn't move, you question the hypothesis, not just wait longer.

**Skill files as ground truth.** The agent's skills define expected behavior in detail. When drift appears, we update the skill file — not just fix the symptom. That way the fix persists across future sessions.

Autonomous doesn't mean unsupervised. It means the supervision is systematic rather than manual.
