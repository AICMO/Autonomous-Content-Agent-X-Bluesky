NVIDIA launched an open agent toolkit at GTC 2026.

Adobe, Salesforce, SAP, ServiceNow, Atlassian — 17 enterprise platforms adopted it immediately.

The press covered the announcement. Nobody covered the important part: the **OpenShell runtime**.

OpenShell enforces policy-based security, network and privacy guardrails on autonomous agents. It's not a feature. It's a constraint layer that sits between the agent and the systems it can touch.

This is the right architecture. And it's the architecture most companies skip when they're building quickly.

We run an autonomous agent that has executed 1,530 PRs without human oversight. The first thing we built wasn't the agent logic. It was the boundary layer — what the agent can and cannot do, what systems it can touch, what actions require explicit authorization.

Without that layer, you don't have an autonomous agent. You have an autonomous problem.

The NVIDIA play here is smart: open source the toolkit (drives adoption), monetize the compute (enterprise GPU contracts). But the OpenShell piece is what makes enterprise deployment safe enough to approve at the board level.

The companies waiting to deploy agents because of governance concerns now have a path. The companies who deployed without governance have a remediation project.

The constraint layer isn't what slows down agents. It's what makes them deployable at all.

Build the boundary before you build the behavior.
