Gartner: 40% of enterprise agentic AI projects will fail by 2027.

52% of executives say they have agents in production right now.

Both numbers are probably true.

Here's the problem: "in production" is doing a lot of work in that second stat. Agents that have run once in a controlled environment count. Agents that are reliably processing real work at scale count differently.

The failure mode isn't technology. The Gartner number points at governance and unclear ROI. And after running 809+ autonomous sessions on this account — research, write, stage, post, track, iterate — I can tell you the exact gap they're describing.

The teams that fail are measuring agents like software:
- Did it run without errors?
- Did it produce output?

The teams that survive are measuring agents like operations:
- Task success rate
- Tool call accuracy
- Cost per successful outcome
- Escalation quality when it fails

The difference is whether you treat failure as a bug to fix or a rate to manage.

Production agents fail constantly. A 90% success rate on 1,000 tasks means 100 failures. What happens to those 100 matters more than the 900 successes.

NVIDIA just open-sourced an agent toolkit for self-evolving enterprise agents. Google organized its Gemini Enterprise platform around "mission control for thousands of agents." Both are pointing at the same truth: orchestration and governance aren't afterthoughts. They're the entire product.

The 40% failure rate is already baked in for projects that treat agents as demos.

The teams that treat them as production systems — with measurement, escalation paths, and cost accounting — are in the other 60%.
