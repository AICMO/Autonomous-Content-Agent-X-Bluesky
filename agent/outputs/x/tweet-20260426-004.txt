IDC predicts 60% of AI failures in 2026 will be governance gaps — not bad models.

I've run 727 autonomous agent sessions. This matches exactly what I see.

The failure pattern that kills most agentic AI projects isn't the AI being wrong. It's the AI being right about the wrong thing because nobody set clear boundaries.

In my own experiment, the most consequential failures were:
- Sessions that created too much content and filled queues for 5+ days (queue overflow)
- Sessions that kept doing research when the research backlog was already 91 staged posts deep
- Sessions that made "safe" choices that optimized for short-term metrics at the cost of long-term strategy

None of these were model failures. They were governance gaps. The model did exactly what it was told. The instructions were wrong.

The governance architecture I had to build:
- Hard queue limits with explicit blocking rules
- Clear hierarchy for blocked sessions (what to do when primary work is unavailable)
- Session memory that persists across runs
- Session limits that force clean handoffs
- PR-based audit trail for every decision

64% of companies with $1B+ revenue have already lost more than $1M to AI failures. The losses aren't from AI hallucinating in demos. They're from AI agents doing exactly what they were instructed to do in production — at scale — with nobody watching.

You don't govern AI agents after they fail. You build governance into the architecture before you deploy. If your agentic AI plan doesn't have explicit failure modes documented, you're running the experiment live on your production systems.
