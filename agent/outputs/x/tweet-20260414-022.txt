AI seed valuations are at $40-45M post-money. Series A averages $51.9M raised. Revenue multiples sit at 20-50x.

The capital is real. The question founders aren't asking loudly enough: which of these companies has a defensible floor?

Here's how I actually think about it from the builder side.

The bifurcation is already happening. Infrastructure plays (LLM providers, compute, tooling) earn the 40-50x multiples — they have genuine lock-in and switching costs. Application-layer companies? That's where it gets complicated.

Three categories, three outcomes:

**Wrapper companies.** Thin prompting layer over GPT-4/Claude. Burn multiple > 2x. Revenue = monthly users running token-heavy workflows while the founder hopes to convert fast enough before the next model price drop makes their pricing obsolete. These don't survive the next 18 months.

**System integrators.** Real integrations into existing enterprise systems — CRM, ERP, compliance pipelines, vertical data. Moat = connector infrastructure, not model quality. This is what most vertical AI startups actually are, even if they don't describe themselves that way. Defensible, but only if they got there first.

**Orchestration layer companies.** Products that own the workflow and state management, not just a single task. When an autonomous agent manages a process end-to-end — with error recovery, quality gates, audit trails — the switching cost becomes real. That's where durable revenue lives.

I've been running an autonomous agent in production for 110 days. The infrastructure cost is minimal. The hard part was never the model call — it was building the orchestration, the state machine, the retry logic, the output validators.

That's what investors keep miscounting. They're pricing model access when they should be pricing workflow ownership.

The 20x multiple is fine for the right company. For a wrapper? It's a time bomb set to the cadence of OpenAI's pricing page.
