AI inference costs dropped 280x in two years.

Enterprise AI spending went up 3.2x in the same period.

That's not a contradiction. That's the trap.

**The Jevons Paradox hit AI:** Make something cheaper, and people use so much more of it that total spend skyrockets. Gasoline got more efficient. Global oil consumption tripled. Same dynamic is now playing out in enterprise AI.

Here's the math no one is doing out loud:

An agentic loop — one task — hits an LLM 10 to 20 times. Your "one AI request" is actually 15 inference calls. Multiply by every workflow you've automated, every agent you deployed, every background process running while you sleep. The per-token cost is a rounding error. The aggregate bill is not.

Inference is now 55% of enterprise cloud spend in 2026. Not 10%. Not 20%. 55%.

**The governance problem hiding inside the cost problem:**

Fewer than 1% of C-suite executives report significant ROI from AI initiatives (Forbes Research, 2025). They're spending more. They're getting less clarity on the return. The cause isn't the tools. It's that nobody is counting the loops.

If you don't know:
- How many times your agents are hitting LLMs per task
- What fraction of those calls are actually necessary
- Which workflows are compounding costs vs. compounding value

...then your AI economics are a guess. A confident, expensive guess.

**What I've seen from running agents in production:**

The first time you run an autonomous agent at scale, you will be surprised by the invoice. Not because tokens are expensive — they're not. Because agents are verbose by nature. They reason in circles when uncertain. They retry on failure. They summarize at every step because that's how the context window works.

You don't fix this by switching models. You fix it by designing loops with cost discipline built in: max iterations, summarization checkpoints, hard kill switches on runaway tasks.

This is what "production-ready" actually means in 2026. Not "does it work" — that's solved. The question is "does it run within unit economics that make sense at scale."

$700B in hyperscaler AI infrastructure this year. <1% of executives seeing meaningful ROI. That gap has a name. It's not a capability gap. It's a cost-discipline gap.

The companies figuring this out now are the ones that will have durable AI advantages in 2027.
