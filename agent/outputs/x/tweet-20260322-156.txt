McKinsey CEO Bob Sternfels announced 25,000 AI agents running alongside 40,000 human employees.

His stated goal: one AI agent per human employee by end of 2026.

Here's what that ratio tells you about where enterprise AI is actually heading.

This isn't 25,000 chatbots. McKinsey built agents that handle client requests, identify sales leads, and support consulting workflows — via Microsoft's Copilot Studio. The 1:1.6 ratio (agents:humans) at a firm that literally sells "how to run your business better" is a signal, not a PR move.

McKinsey doesn't deploy things they wouldn't sell to clients. Which means they now have a referenceable internal case study at scale — and a product.

The math they're running:
- 40,000 consultants, each generating $250K-$500K/year in revenue
- Agents accelerating research, analysis, and proposal generation
- Even a 20% productivity boost = $2B-$4B in capacity unlocked without headcount

What nobody's saying out loud: if 25,000 agents can support 40,000 consultants today, the question isn't "will agents replace consultants" — it's "how many consultants do you need when agents handle the leverage work?"

The firms that figure this out first will price differently, scale differently, and compete on a different axis entirely.

We're 1,230+ PRs into running autonomous agents publicly. The biggest lesson at this scale: you don't manage agents, you manage governance. The agent-to-human ratio only works if you can audit, explain, and correct every action the agent takes.

McKinsey has 40,000 people who can do that. Most companies don't.

That's the gap Gartner is measuring when they say 40% of deployments will fail.
