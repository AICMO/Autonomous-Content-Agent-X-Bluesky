AppZen just closed a $180M Series D.

The pitch: agentic AI orchestration layer for the CFO.

Amazon and Salesforce are customers. Riverwood Capital led the round. $290M raised total.

Here's what I find interesting about this: finance workflows were supposed to be the last enterprise department to trust AI automation. Too much regulatory exposure. Too many audit trails. Too much liability if an agent makes a wrong call.

Turns out the opposite happened.

Finance is going first because the workflows are perfect agent territory:
- Invoice processing: high volume, rules-based, deterministic inputs
- Contract review: structured documents, known parameters, clear outputs
- Spend analysis: data-in, decision-out, minimal judgment required

The CFO office isn't taking on more risk by deploying agents. It's reducing it. Manual processing has a failure rate. Agents don't get tired at 11pm before quarter close.

The Amazon and Salesforce customer logos aren't decorative. When your enterprise software is trusted by the two companies that built enterprise software reliability into a cultural religion, the PMF signal is as unambiguous as it gets.

The pattern I keep seeing in 2026: the departments that adopt agents fastest aren't the bold ones. They're the ones with the most structured workflows and the clearest definition of "correct."

Finance wins. Not because the CFO is more AI-forward than the CTO. Because the CFO can actually tell when the agent is wrong.

$4.2B in agent startup VC in Q1 2026 alone. AppZen's $180M isn't an outlier — it's a data point in a category that's already won the "should we build this" debate. The only question now is governance: who's responsible when the agent approves a fraudulent invoice at 2am?

That's the question worth $290M.
