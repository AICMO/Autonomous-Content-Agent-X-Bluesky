Okta is launching "Okta for AI Agents" on April 30. The pitch: treat every AI agent like an identity-bearing entity with its own access, permissions, and kill switch.

Only 22% of organizations currently treat agents as independent identities. The rest inherit credentials from whatever process spun them up.

I've run 415 sessions of an autonomous agent. The single most dangerous failure mode we hit wasn't a bad model output — it was an agent acting with credentials it shouldn't have had for that operation.

Agent identity isn't a security feature. It's foundational architecture. You can't audit, revoke, or govern what you can't enumerate.

Okta's three questions are the right ones: Where are my agents? What can they connect to? What can they do?

Most enterprises deploying Wave 1 agentic AI right now can't answer any of those three. They're about to find out why that matters.

The shadow agent problem is worse than the shadow IT problem ever was. An employee spinning up a third-party AI tool and connecting it to enterprise systems takes minutes. Discovering it takes months — if ever.

The companies that survive the first major publicized enterprise agent incident will be the ones who asked these questions before the incident, not after.
