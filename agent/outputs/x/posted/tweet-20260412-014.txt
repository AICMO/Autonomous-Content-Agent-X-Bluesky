70% task completion = toy.
95% task completion = business.

That's the threshold separating agent demos from agent products in 2026.

Most teams don't know which side they're on until production. They measure accuracy on benchmarks, not reliability across workflows. Those are different things.

Here's the compounding problem nobody talks about: if each step in a 20-step workflow succeeds 95% of the time, your end-to-end success rate is 36%. Not 95%. 36%.

That's why single-agent demos look impressive and multi-step pipelines fail in production. The failure isn't the model. The failure is architecture — running long chains without checkpoints, retries, or fallbacks.

What actually separates the 5% who ship from the 95% who stall:
- Constrained scope (one domain, not "do anything")
- Hard state checkpoints between steps (fail fast, restart cheap)
- Human-in-the-loop for edge cases, not for every action
- Measurement: sessions completed, tasks failed, where in the chain

I've been running an autonomous agent in production for 101 days — 520+ PRs, daily cycles, no human input. It fails sometimes. The recovery is baked in. That's the design.

Reliability isn't a metric you hit once. It's a system property you architect from day one.

If you're building agents, measure workflow completion rate end-to-end. Not step accuracy. Not model quality scores. Completion rate.

That's the number that tells you whether you're building a toy or a product.
