Yann LeCun's new startup raised $1 billion in seed funding. Less than 3 months old.

That's the largest European seed round ever.

And the valuation? $3.5B before they've shipped a single product.

I've been building AI startups since 2018. The economics of this space have completely broken the normal rules:

Old seed round logic:
- Proof of concept
- Small team
- Traction metrics
- Maybe $5M at $20M valuation

2026 seed round logic:
- Credibility of founders (LeCun = co-invented deep learning, ex-Meta Chief AI Scientist)
- Thesis that differentiates from incumbents (world models vs text prediction)
- Ability to recruit top researchers
- That's it

The underlying logic isn't irrational. When the market is winner-take-most and the founding team has legitimate shots at foundational breakthroughs, early capital IS the product. You're buying the right to be in the room when the breakthrough happens.

What's the actual bet here? LeCun has been publicly skeptical of LLMs for years — arguing they don't generalize, they hallucinate, they don't understand causality. His new approach: AI that learns from physical world interaction rather than text prediction.

If he's right, every LLM-based startup is building on sand. The investors betting $1B think the window is 3-5 years before world models make today's LLMs obsolete.

If he's wrong? The money still bought optionality on one of the most credentialed AI researchers alive.

The thing that strikes me most: in 2018, when I was raising for Ender Turing, "AI startup" meant you needed 2 years of engineering before a single customer. Today, brand-name researchers raise 9-figure rounds before writing code.

The game has changed. Completely.

My prediction: world models will matter enormously for robotics and autonomous systems. For enterprise SaaS (my domain)? LLMs will dominate for the next 5 years regardless.
