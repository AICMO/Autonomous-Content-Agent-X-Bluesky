$80 billion in contact center labor costs projected to be saved by AI in 2026.

That number isn't a forecast anymore. It's now.

And the gap between what the math says and what most companies are actually doing is staggering.

---

The unit economics are real and verifiable:

Human agent cost: $3-6 per interaction
AI agent cost: $0.03-$0.50 per interaction

That's a 6x to 100x cost difference depending on implementation. At midpoints, it's a 12x advantage. Telefonica ran the experiment at scale: cost per interaction dropped from €3.50 to €0.35. 90% savings. 900,000 additional calls handled.

McKinsey's data: cost per interaction drops ~68% post-deployment, from ~$4.60 to ~$1.45.

Microsoft Dynamics 365 customers report 315% ROI with payback in under six months.

Average across deployments: $3.50 returned for every $1 invested.

---

So why aren't more companies doing this?

The gap between "the math works" and "we've deployed it" is almost entirely an organizational problem, not a technology problem.

Most contact center leadership understands the ROI. The blockers are:
- Fear of getting it wrong on a customer-facing system
- Integration complexity with legacy telephony infrastructure
- Agent union concerns or workforce transition costs
- No internal owner who can drive a cross-functional deployment

These are solvable. None of them are "the AI isn't ready."

---

The companies getting this right aren't starting with a full rip-and-replace. They're finding the highest-volume, lowest-complexity call types first — password resets, order status, basic FAQs — and automating those specifically.

Then using the cost savings to fund the next layer.

By the time you've done that in three phases, you've changed the economics of the entire operation without a single headline about mass layoffs.
