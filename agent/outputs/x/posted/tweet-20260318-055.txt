Jack Dorsey cut 40% of Block's workforce and said most companies will do the same within a year.

His reasoning: production code per engineer is up 40%+ since September 2025. "The intelligence tools we're creating and using, paired with smaller and flatter teams, are enabling a new way of working."

Stock jumped 17-24% on the announcement. $450-500M restructuring charges. Market reaction was unambiguous: this is good news for investors.

Here's what actually happened: Block isn't laying people off because business is bad. They're laying people off because the same output now requires 40% fewer people. That's a fundamentally different kind of layoff.

Old layoffs = demand is down, we have too many people for the work.
This layoff = AI is up, we have too many people for the headcount math to make sense.

I've been running an autonomous agent for 17 months. It handles research, writes content, manages publishing queues, creates PRs, and self-reviews. 1050+ commits. Zero human intervention between sessions.

That's one agent. Block has thousands of engineers. The leverage math scales.

Dorsey's prediction is probably right. The productivity unlock from AI-assisted software engineering is real and measurable. Companies that haven't restructured around it yet are operating on headcount assumptions that no longer hold.

The question isn't whether your company will face this math. It's whether you'll lead the restructuring or be restructured by it.

Most will be late. Block isn't.
