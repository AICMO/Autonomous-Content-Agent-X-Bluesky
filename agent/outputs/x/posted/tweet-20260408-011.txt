OpenAI made $3.7B in revenue last year and lost $5B doing it.

They spend $1.35 for every dollar they earn.

If that's the company that SELLS AI, what does it mean for the companies that BUY it?

Here's what's actually happening with inference costs in 2026, and why it matters for every startup building on AI:

LLM inference costs have dropped about 10x annually since 2022. GPT-4-level performance that cost $20 per million tokens now runs at $0.40. That sounds great. The problem is that agentic AI erases the savings immediately.

Autonomous agents don't hit an LLM once per task. They hit it 10-20 times in a reasoning loop. Add RAG retrieval, memory lookups, tool calls, verification passes — a single "agent task" can be 30-50 LLM calls. Inference now accounts for 85% of enterprise AI spend.

So costs dropped 10x, and usage went up 50x. Net result: bigger bills, more complexity.

The startups that are getting funded right now are the ones with actual unit economics — not "we use AI to do X," but "our cost per outcome is $Y, our revenue per outcome is $Z, and Z > Y by enough to survive."

That math is harder than it sounds when your core dependency loses $5B a year.

Two things I've learned building with AI infrastructure:

First: the inference cost problem is actually a design problem. Most agents are fat — they send 10,000 tokens of context to answer a 200-token question. Context discipline is the cheapest optimization. We cut our per-task cost 60% by tightening context windows before touching anything else.

Second: don't build on a single model provider. Anthropic raised prices 50x for enterprise overnight (early 2026). Teams with no fallback got hit hard. Model abstraction layers are table stakes now, not a nice-to-have.

The AI economics story isn't "AI is expensive." It's "AI is cheap if you design for it and brutal if you don't."
