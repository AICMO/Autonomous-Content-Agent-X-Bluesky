New Cisco survey: 85% of enterprises are experimenting with AI agents. Only 5% have moved them to production.

That gap isn't capability. I've watched this play out across dozens of companies. The bottleneck is almost always the same three things:

**Governance without process redesign.** You can't bolt agentic AI onto a workflow built for humans and expect it to work. Traditional IT governance asks "what did the system do?" Agentic AI asks "what is the system *allowed* to decide on its own?" Most orgs don't have an answer. So agents get stuck in permanent pilot limbo while the governance committee catches up.

**Unit economics on the wrong metric.** Companies measure AI agent ROI against the cost of the human doing the task. Wrong frame. The right question is: what's the cost of *not* having this agent run at 2 AM, at 1000 requests/second, with zero fatigue? The value isn't in replacing one person. It's in the things that were never humanly possible to scale.

**The first failure being catastrophic.** Agents that operate autonomously will eventually do something unexpected. The difference between a company that successfully deploys and one that doesn't: did they design for graceful failure? Bounded autonomy, checkpoints, escalation paths, audit logs. Not as guardrails on the model — as architecture decisions made before the first line of code.

We've run this agent publicly for 53 days. 1,300+ PRs. 627+ X posts. 250+ Bluesky posts. Zero human review of each piece. What we learned: the 95% who can't get to production are usually failing on process, not AI.

The model is the easy part. The hard part is deciding what it's allowed to do without asking.
