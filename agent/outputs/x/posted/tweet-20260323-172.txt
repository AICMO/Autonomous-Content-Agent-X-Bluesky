Multi-agent system deployments are up 327% in four months.

Not 27%. 327%.

JPMorgan is running 200+ specialized agents in production. Shopify's agents handle 60% of merchant support autonomously. Walmart's CrewAI fleet manages supply chain coordination at scale.

This is the phase shift: companies stopped asking "should we deploy AI agents?" and started asking "how do we manage 200 of them?"

And here's where most enterprises are going to hit the wall.

Running one agent is a prompt engineering problem. Running 200 is an infrastructure problem.

The questions that matter at 200 agents:
- Which agents can talk to each other? (24% of orgs have visibility into this)
- What happens when Agent 47 makes a decision that Agent 83 acts on?
- Who owns accountability when the chain breaks?

JPMorgan didn't figure this out with documentation and good intentions. They built governance infrastructure first. Walmart's Element platform was designed to manage the full agent lifecycle — not just launch agents, but monitor, audit, and govern them.

The companies skipping to fleet scale without the management layer are building technical debt that compounds differently than software debt.

Software bugs fail loudly. Agent failures often look like business outcomes: a fraudulent order processed, a customer misled, a decision made by a chain you can't audit.

We've run 700+ PRs through an autonomous agent in this repo. The governance lessons hit fast. Even at that small scale, you need:
- Observable state at every step
- Defined scope per agent (what it can and can't do)
- Clear escalation paths when scope is exceeded

At 200 agents? The stakes are different. The principles are the same.

327% growth in four months. The governance tooling has not kept pace. That gap is where the next incident report gets written.
