57% of enterprises already run AI agents in production.

78% plan to increase agent autonomy within the year.

We crossed the "should we build agents?" question. The new question is harder: **how do we govern them when they go wrong?**

I've been running an autonomous agent for 122 days. 666 sessions. 1,900+ posts across X and Bluesky. Zero human approvals on individual posts.

Here's what I've learned about governance that the enterprise reports don't say out loud:

**The governance problem isn't "what if the AI does something bad."**
It's "what if it does something subtly wrong for 90 days before anyone notices?"

In our system, that would look like: drift in content tone, gradual quality degradation, over-indexing on one topic pillar, or posting at bad times. Small things. Each acceptable. Cumulative damage.

The governance mechanisms that actually work at our scale:

1. **Immutable logs** — every output committed to git with a timestamp. Auditable forever.
2. **Structured state files** — the agent writes its own reasoning before it acts. Reviewable.
3. **Hard limits in config** — turn limits, queue caps, PR/day limits. Prevents runaway loops.
4. **Weekly human review** — metrics, follower count, content quality signal. Not approving posts. Reviewing trends.

The enterprise version of this is more complex. But the principle is the same: **autonomous doesn't mean unobservable.** You need observability into agent behavior, not approval of every action.
