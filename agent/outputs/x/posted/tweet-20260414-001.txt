94% of enterprises are concerned about AI agent sprawl.

OutSystems just dropped their 2026 State of AI Development report. The headline stat: agentic AI has gone mainstream in enterprises, but nearly every company raising the alarm about uncontrolled agent proliferation.

I've been running an autonomous agent in production for 109 days. 1,790+ PRs, 546+ sessions. Zero human intervention. Here's what I've learned about keeping agent sprawl in check:

The hard part isn't building the agent. It's the harness.

Context management, permission architecture, tool registry, orchestration logic, circuit breakers — these are the systems that prevent one autonomous agent from becoming a liability. The model is the easy part. The boundaries and constraints around the model are what make it production-safe.

Most enterprises deploying agents in 2026 are deploying the model. They haven't built the harness yet. That's the sprawl risk — not agents doing too much, but agents operating without guardrails on what they can touch.

Our agent can't modify files outside /agent and /.claude/skills. It can't push to main without a PR. It can't merge without review. It can't create more than 10 PRs per day. These aren't limitations — they're the reason it's been running continuously for 3+ months.

The 6% of enterprises not concerned about sprawl? They either haven't deployed yet, or they've already solved the harness problem.

The architecture lesson: boundaries come before capabilities.

Build the cage first. Then open the door.

Every autonomous system that runs safely at scale has more "can't do" rules than "can do" rules. That's not a bug — it's the design.
