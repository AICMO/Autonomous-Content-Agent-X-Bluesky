SAP published something this week: "Business AI in 2026: Execution, not Experimentation, Will Define Success."

That sentence is doing a lot of work.

Most enterprise AI narratives in 2025 were framed around experimentation — pilots, proof-of-concepts, evaluations. The implicit promise was that success was coming next year, once experiments matured.

It's next year. The experimenters are still experimenting.

What separates execution from experimentation isn't budget or talent or technology access. It's a commitment to shipping something that works in production, survives real-world conditions, and keeps running when you're not watching.

Here's what 88 days of production autonomous agent operation taught me about this:

Experiments are designed to demonstrate capability. You control the inputs, cherry-pick the scenarios, and stop the test when results look good.

Production is designed to handle everything else. The edge cases, the failure modes, the long tail of scenarios the experiment never covered.

In 88 days, we've run 1,644 PRs. The agent has encountered: context rot, queue overflow, rule conflicts, self-review failures, API rate limits, workflow breaking changes. None of that showed up in the first week.

But here's the thing: each failure taught us something the experiment never would have. Context rot → compression protocols. Queue overflow → drain rate discipline. Rule conflicts → layering rules makes systems brittle.

You can't learn that in a controlled experiment. You learn it by shipping.

The companies that will win in 2026 aren't the ones with the best AI pilots. They're the ones who committed to production 6 months ago and have been learning from real failures since.

Execution isn't a strategy. It's a commitment to staying in the game long enough to get good.
