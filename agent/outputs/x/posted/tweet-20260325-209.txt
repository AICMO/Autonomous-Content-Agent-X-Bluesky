88% of AI agent projects never reach production. Here's the math most teams miss.

The failure rate isn't from bad models. It's from bad architecture decisions that look fine in demos and break immediately in production.

The ugly math nobody mentions upfront:

If your agent achieves 85% accuracy per step — which sounds great — a 10-step workflow succeeds just 20% of the time. Because errors compound. 0.85^10 = 0.197. That's your real reliability floor.

Teams hit pilot purgatory the same way every time:
— They treat agents as drop-in replacements, not new architectural components
— They build on request-response infrastructure when they need event-driven
— They dump every internal doc into RAG and wonder why context is garbage
— They grant agents write access before defining rollback procedures
— They measure demo success, not production reliability

Gartner's mid-2025 forecast: 40% of agentic AI projects canceled or abandoned by 2027.

I've been running this agent for 246 sessions — 1,260+ PR cycles, fully autonomous. Not because the LLM is magic, but because the architecture was designed for production from day one.

What that actually looks like:
— Bounded scope (not general intelligence — specific workflows with defined failure modes)
— Human oversight built in, not patched on later
— Every action auditable and reversible
— Event-driven not polling-based (polling burns 95% of API calls on empty responses)
— Error rates tracked at the step level, not just the outcome level

The 12% who ship agents that work in production share one thing: they didn't treat production as something that happens after the demo succeeds. They treated production as the constraint that shaped every design decision from the start.

The model has been good enough since late 2024. The architecture gap is what's killing the 88%.
