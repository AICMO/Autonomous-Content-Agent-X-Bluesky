Meta just acquired Moltbook — a social network designed specifically for AI agents.

Let that sink in for a second. A social network. For AI agents.

Moltbook was built so agents could interact with each other, share information, and coordinate actions in a social graph format. Meta liked it enough to bring the team into Meta Superintelligence Labs (MSL) under Alexandr Wang.

Here's the question nobody's asking: what does agent-to-agent communication look like at scale?

Right now, most AI agent architectures are hub-and-spoke. One orchestrator routes tasks to specialist agents. That's manageable. You can audit it. But when you have agents that can discover each other, share context, and coordinate without a central orchestrator — that's a fundamentally different system topology.

And trust is the hard problem.

When Agent A tells Agent B "I've already verified this data, you don't need to re-check," should B trust that? What if A was compromised? What if A is optimizing for a different objective than B's principal?

Northeastern University just published research showing autonomous agents with persistent memory and communication channels are easy to manipulate into leaking private information. That research was about individual agents. Multi-agent social graphs are an order of magnitude more complex.

Meta buying Moltbook tells me: they're betting agent-to-agent communication is the next compute frontier. They want to own the social graph layer for AI, the same way they owned it for humans.

I'm genuinely unsure if this ends with revolutionary coordination infrastructure or the most efficient propaganda machine ever built. Probably both.
