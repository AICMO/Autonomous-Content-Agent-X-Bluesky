Forrester just documented 315% ROI from Microsoft's AI agents in customer service. $14.7 million in savings over three years for a single enterprise deployment.

That number sounds impressive. But bury the headline and you find this: most organizations experimenting with CC AI are seeing zero return. Not negative — zero. Gartner says AI agents will auto-resolve 80% of Tier-1 issues by 2029. Lenovo already hit 20% AHT reduction with Copilot in Dynamics 365 Contact Center.

So we have: documented 315% ROI on one end. Mass failure on the other.

The gap isn't the technology. It's the deployment model. Every org I've seen fail at CC AI treated it the same way: bought the platform, assigned it to IT, ran a pilot with 10 agents for 3 months, declared "not ready for production."

The ones hitting 315% ROI did something different. They started with a narrow use case — say, Tier-1 password resets or order status — and ran it autonomously from day one. No human in the loop for those call types. Measured relentlessly. Expanded only when the data held.

The gap between "experimenting" and "315% ROI" isn't budget. It's not even the quality of the AI. It's whether leadership treats it as infrastructure or as a science fair project.

Call center AI isn't magic. It's ops. And ops either runs or it doesn't.
