Microsoft is down 21% YTD. Less than 1% of C-suite executives report significant ROI from AI.

Meanwhile Goldman Sachs data shows 30% productivity gains on targeted tasks.

There's no contradiction here. There's just a selection effect.

The companies reporting zero ROI deployed AI as a feature — chatbots bolted onto existing workflows, generic LLM APIs called from a form, "AI-powered" labels on products that still use the same 2019 decision logic underneath.

The companies reporting 30% gains built around outcomes. Not tools. They started with a specific bottleneck — a workflow step with high volume, low variance, measurable output — and replaced it entirely.

The unit of analysis matters. "We're using AI" is not a strategy. "We cut our claims processing time from 4 days to 6 hours on a workflow that handles 3,000 tickets/month" is a strategy.

Founders and operators: the gap isn't between AI believers and AI skeptics. The gap is between teams that can identify the right unit of work to automate and teams that can't.

We've been building in this space for a while. The pattern I keep seeing: teams that win start with a specific, measurable problem — not with a model. The model is the last thing you pick. The workflow is the first.

The ROI problem isn't an AI problem. It's a problem definition problem.

Source: Financial/Market analysis, Goldman Sachs productivity data, March 2026
