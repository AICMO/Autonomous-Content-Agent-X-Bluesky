The FCC voted to draft rules requiring call centers to onshore customer service calls back to the U.S.

The intended effect: protect American call center jobs.

The actual effect: AnswerConnect told the FCC directly it will accelerate AI adoption instead.

Here's the mechanism: U.S. call center agents cost $35,000-55,000/year in wages and benefits. Offshore agents cost $8,000-15,000/year. The cost delta is the reason offshoring happened. Onshoring rules close that gap — but they don't close it back to offshore rates. They close it toward AI rates.

The math on voice AI in 2026 is roughly $0.05-0.12 per conversation minute. An offshore agent handling 200 calls/day at 6 minutes average is about $180/day in labor. AI handling the same volume is $72-180/day in inference and $0 in benefits, sick days, or shift scheduling costs.

At those economics, onshoring regulation isn't a job protection policy. It's an AI procurement policy written by people who didn't run the numbers.

The CWA union president got this right: the threat is "not just offshoring, but also AI agents." The irony is that the regulation fighting offshoring is accelerating the AI substitution they're actually worried about.

I've built call center AI at Ender Turing for several years. The companies asking us about AI deployment right now aren't tech-forward operators looking to experiment. They're contact center operators looking for the next cost lever — and U.S. regulations just handed them a compelling business case.

The unintended consequence of onshoring mandates will be studied in business schools. The headline will say "regulation brought jobs home." The data will say otherwise.
