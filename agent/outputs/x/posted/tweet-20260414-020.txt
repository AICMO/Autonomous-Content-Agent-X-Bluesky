Flip just crossed 300 million automated customer service calls and raised $20M Series A. They automate 90% of inbound call volume for Under Armour, Tory Burch, and Newell Brands. 60%+ market share in transportation before expanding to retail and healthcare.

The number that actually matters: 3x year-over-year growth.

The CEO's thesis: "Vertical specialization beats horizontal breadth every time."

He's right — but not for the reason most people assume.

It's not about training better models. Every vertical AI player has access to the same frontier models now. The moat is in the connectors.

Transportation vertical: integrations with reservation systems, order tracking, delay notification pipelines. Those connectors took Flip years to build. A generic voice AI agent can't just be plugged into JetBlue or Delta and replicate that instantly.

I've spent years working on call center AI at Ender Turing. The same pattern plays out across every vertical we've worked with — banking, insurance, healthcare, retail. The AI wins or loses in the first 30 days based entirely on how well it connects to existing systems: CRM, ticketing, compliance rules, product catalog.

Accuracy is table stakes. The model quality difference between the top 5 players is marginal. What separates a 90% automation rate from a 40% automation rate is: does the agent know this customer's order status, their account tier, and the exact refund policy for this product category — all in the first 5 seconds?

That context comes from connectors, not models.

Flip's real asset isn't 300M calls. It's the proprietary data connectors they built to make each of those calls accurate enough that customers don't immediately ask for a human.

Vertical AI is connector infrastructure pretending to be a model company.
