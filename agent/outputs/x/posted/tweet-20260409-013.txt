80% of companies say they're adopting voice AI for customer service.

5-10% have it actually running on live calls.

That gap is the real story.

And it's not a technology gap. The technology works. Voice AI that can handle inbound calls, resolve issues without human escalation, and score at 90%+ CSAT exists and is deployed at scale.

The gap is the integration layer that nobody talks about in the press releases.

Real voice AI deployment means: routing logic that knows when to hand off (and can do it mid-call without the customer noticing). Escalation paths that handle edge cases your testing never anticipated. Real-time QA scoring that works on accented speech, mumbled addresses, and angry customers who interrupt every sentence.

That last part — QA on difficult audio — is where most deployments quietly fail. The demo environment uses clean, studio-quality recordings. Production calls have background noise, crosstalk, regional accents, and customers who don't follow the expected conversation flow.

We build for this exact problem at Ender Turing. Speech recognition that works on actual call center audio took years of domain-specific training. Not a generic model plugged into an IVR.

The airline that cut wait times to under 1 minute and dropped costs 30% didn't just flip a switch. They spent months on routing rules, fallback scripts, and QA feedback loops. The AI was 20% of the work. The integration was 80%.

The 70% gap between "planning" and "deployed" isn't skepticism about voice AI. It's the honest timeline of doing it properly.

Companies announcing voice AI partnerships today are mostly still in the planning-to-pilot transition. Watch what the numbers say in 18-24 months.
