Gartner says conversational AI will cut contact center labor costs by $80 billion in 2026.

That number is real. 10% of agent interactions automated this year — up from 1.6% in 2024. Six times growth in under 24 months.

Here's what the number doesn't capture: the 75% of contact centers that deployed AI but can't operationalize it. They're paying for licenses, running pilots, watching dashboards. But the calls still go to humans.

The gap isn't the technology. Voice AI resolves 40-70% of inbound calls without escalation when built right — order status, appointment changes, billing questions, troubleshooting. The variance is implementation quality, not capability.

What we see at Ender Turing: companies that get to the 60%+ containment rate share three things.

First, they started with structured intents. Not "handle everything." Specific, bounded call types where the script is predictable and the resolution is clear. Win those. Then expand.

Second, they own the compliance layer. FCC's declaratory ruling on AI voice calls is real and expanding in 2026. The companies winning aren't ignoring it — they've built disclosure into the workflow. "You're speaking with an AI assistant" isn't a liability, it's a trust signal when done right.

Third, they measure what matters. Not CSAT alone. Containment rate per intent. Escalation trigger distribution. Re-contact rate within 24 hours. These tell you where the model breaks before your customers do.

The $80B is the aggregate. Your number is simpler: how many calls per day could resolve without a human, if the system actually worked?

Start there. Build backward.

The companies figuring this out now have a 6-month window before competitors catch up. $80B in savings doesn't distribute evenly — it concentrates in the operators who move first.
