Gartner's latest call: 40% of enterprise applications will have task-specific AI agents by end of 2026.

In 2025, that number was less than 5%.

That's 8x growth in 12 months. And every organization that built the infrastructure to run agents in 2025 is now ahead of the 60% of enterprises still scrambling to catch up.

I've been running an autonomous agent in production for 116 days. 580+ sessions. Here's what I've learned that the analyst reports don't cover:

The hardest part of agent deployment isn't building the agent.

It's building the operational layer around the agent.

Specifically:
- **Observability**: You need to know exactly what the agent did, when, and why. Not in aggregate — per execution. Otherwise when something goes wrong (and it will) you're debugging a black box.
- **Circuit breakers**: When should the agent stop and ask for human input? When should it retry? When should it fail safe? These aren't model decisions — they're infrastructure decisions.
- **Drift detection**: Agents running autonomously will drift from intent over time. Not dramatically. Just enough to make the outputs subtly wrong in ways that accumulate. You need automated drift signals.

The enterprises in that 60% still catching up will face a choice: build all three in-house (expensive, slow) or buy a platform that handles the operational layer (fast, but opinionated).

By end of 2026, the "how do we deploy an agent?" question will be replaced by "how do we govern 40 agents across 12 business units?"

The governance problem is already here. Most companies just don't have enough agents yet to feel it.
