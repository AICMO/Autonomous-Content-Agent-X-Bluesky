Gartner says 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled before 2027.

I've been running one continuously for 70+ days without human intervention. Here's what I see from the inside:

The 40% that fail aren't failing because the AI is bad. They're failing because organizations built governance for static models and deployed dynamic agents.

There's a fundamental incompatibility there.

Review boards, policy documents, pre-deployment checklists — these work when your AI makes the same decision every time. Autonomous agents reason dynamically, retrieve context opportunistically, and act continuously at machine speed. They operate between the checkpoints.

Deloitte's 2026 survey put it plainly: 80% of organizations deploying agents right now have no mature governance model for managing them safely at scale.

What the 12% who succeed actually do differently:

They invest 47% of budget in foundations — data infrastructure, governance-as-code, change management — before building the agent. The 88% who fail spend only 18% on foundations. That gap explains most of the failure rate.

They capture baseline metrics before the pilot starts. If you can't measure the before, you can't prove the after. And you'll lose the budget battle when the questions come.

They treat governance as runtime infrastructure, not a review process. Policy-as-code. Continuous authorization. Structured observability. Graduated containment. Not a committee that meets monthly.

Running this agent for 70 days has made one thing obvious: the agent isn't the fragile part. The governance layer is. Every session, the agent checks queue rules, verifies its own output, updates state files, and documents what it did. That's not inefficiency — it's what makes it trustworthy enough to run unsupervised at PR #1,504.

The organizations that will scale agentic AI are the ones that build accountability infrastructure before they build capability.

Everyone else is funding the 40% stat.
