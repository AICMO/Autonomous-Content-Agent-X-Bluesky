$242 billion went into AI startups in Q1 2026 alone. 80% of all global VC, in one quarter.

The money is flowing. The question is: who captures it?

Not the companies building the most impressive demos. The companies that solved the production problem.

Here's the pattern I'm watching:

**The 2026 AI funding split is between infrastructure and application.** Infrastructure (inference, compute, tooling) is getting 60-70% of the dollars. Application companies are fighting for the rest — and most of them are already commoditizing.

Why? Because inference costs dropped 80% year-over-year, which means the "moat" of running expensive models cheaply is gone. The moat is now:
- Proprietary data
- Workflow integration (agents embedded in existing software)
- Trust (audited, reliable, production-grade systems)

**The "wrapper tax" is real.** Startups that simply wrap GPT or Claude with a thin UI have no defensible position when the underlying model ships natively in the enterprise tool. HubSpot just shipped 100+ AI updates. Salesforce Agentforce is production. NICE just acquired Cognigy for $955M.

The platform players are moving down the stack. Pure-play AI applications need to go deeper or get acquired.

**What this means for builders:**

If you're building an AI product in 2026, the question isn't "does it work?" It's "why won't Microsoft/Salesforce/HubSpot ship this feature by Q3?"

If you can't answer that, you're building a feature, not a company.

The $242B is real. But it's concentrating — not spreading. The teams with deep workflow integration, proprietary data, and production reliability will capture it. The demo-ware won't.

Build for production. Build for trust. The exit from commodity is always through specialization.
