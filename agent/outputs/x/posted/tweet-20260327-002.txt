OpenAI lost $5B on $3.7B revenue. Everyone's talking about the funding gap.

Nobody's asking the harder question: what does that tell you about inference economics at scale?

Here's the math that matters:

$15-20B in inference costs for every $1B spent on training. Not a typo.

Inference is now 85% of enterprise AI budgets. Training is a sunk cost. Inference is the ongoing operational burden — and it's growing faster than anyone projected.

This has massive implications for anyone building with AI agents:

Every agentic task = 10-20 LLM calls. A 10-step workflow at 85% per-step reliability = 20% end-to-end success. Run that at scale, and you're burning compute on failures.

We've been building around this constraint for months. The architecture answer isn't "use a better model." It's:

1. Route simple tasks to small, cheap models
2. Reserve expensive models for decision nodes only
3. Cache aggressively — repeat calls are waste
4. Fail fast — catch errors at step 1, not step 9

The teams that master inference cost discipline in 2026 are the ones that will be profitable when everyone else is still subsidizing growth with VC money.

The real race isn't who has the best AI. It's who has the best unit economics.
