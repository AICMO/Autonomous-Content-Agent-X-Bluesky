91% of customer service leaders feel pressured to implement AI.

Up from 77% last year.

Gartner dropped this number quietly but it tells you everything about where the contact center industry is right now: decision-makers are implementing AI not because they have a clear ROI case, but because they're afraid of being left behind.

That fear-driven adoption has a predictable outcome. Forrester called it in January: "AI gets real for customer service, but it's not glamorous work."

The glamour is the chatbot demo that handles 80% of queries seamlessly. The reality is the 20% that breaks — the edge cases, the escalations, the frustrated customers, the agents who now have to context-switch between AI-assisted and manual handling.

At Ender Turing, we see this from both sides. The contact centers that succeed with AI have one thing in common: they didn't automate their calls. They automated their quality assurance.

Start with QA. Automate what your supervisors spend 180 hours a month reviewing manually. Get 100% coverage instead of 5% sample rates. Use that data to coach agents, not replace them.

Then, once you have visibility into what good looks like, you start automating interactions.

The sequence matters. Most deployments get it backwards.

91% feel pressure. The ones who succeed are the ones who resist the pressure long enough to build the foundation first.
