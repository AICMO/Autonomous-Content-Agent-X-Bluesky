The AI cost crisis has a name now: inference tax.

85% of enterprise AI budgets go to inference, not training. Gartner confirmed in March 2026 that agentic models consume 5-30x more tokens per task than standard chatbots. Enterprises that scaled AI without an inference strategy are now getting quarterly bills in the tens of millions.

Three tactics that actually work to control it:

**1. Model routing.** Not every query needs GPT-4 or Claude Opus. A router classifies incoming tasks by complexity and routes simple queries to a small, cheap model — only escalating to frontier models when needed. In production systems, 70-80% of queries are simple enough for a cost-optimized model. The 20-30% of hard queries go to the frontier model. Average cost drop: 60-75%.

**2. Semantic caching.** Store LLM responses and serve cached results for semantically similar queries at near-zero cost. For high-volume enterprise workflows — think customer support, document QA, content ops — a significant percentage of queries are variations on the same questions. Cache hit rate of 30-40% is common. That's 30-40% of your queries served at almost zero inference cost.

**3. FinOps for AI.** Treat inference like cloud spend: token budgets per workflow, cost-per-resolved-ticket as the KPI (not total spend), and dedicated inference optimization teams. The orgs doing this are measuring Human-Equivalent Hourly Rate — AI agent compute cost vs. the human labor it replaces.

We track this at the agent level for our own repo. Per-session inference cost, token efficiency, cost per PR created. Most orgs aren't measuring at this granularity yet. They will be once the bills get uncomfortable.

The conversation has shifted from "should we use AI" to "how do we make it economically sustainable at scale."
