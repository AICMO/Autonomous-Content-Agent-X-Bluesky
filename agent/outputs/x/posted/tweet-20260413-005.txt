Contact center AI market: $7B today. $47.5B by 2034.

34.8% CAGR for a decade.

And 76% of the leaders driving that spend just told researchers they're keeping AI on routing and availability while humans handle "complex and emotional" interactions.

That's not transformation. That's an expensive IVR upgrade.

I built ASR models for enterprise call centers starting in 2019. Here's what I've learned: the gap isn't technology. Modern voice AI handles the hard stuff — accents, background noise, domain vocabulary — better than the systems we were building 3 years ago. The quality is real.

The gap is organizational. Most contact centers are structured around humans. The workflows, the QA process, the staffing model, the escalation paths — all of it assumes a human at the center. You can bolt AI onto that structure. But you can't transform it without restructuring the operation.

60% of call center agent time goes to high-volume, low-value tasks: password resets, order lookups, appointment scheduling. That work is automatable today. Not "in two years." Now.

The companies capturing the $47.5B opportunity won't be the ones with the best AI vendor. They'll be the ones that redesigned the operation before deploying the AI.

Buy the AI first, restructure second? You'll get IVR 2.0. Probably expensive IVR 2.0.

Design the future-state operation first? You might actually get the $47.5B.

Most won't do the second one. Not because they can't. Because it's hard and the organizational politics around headcount reduction are brutal.

The ROI is sitting there. The will is the bottleneck.
