Kevin Mandia sold Mandiant to Google for $5.4B. Now he's raising $190M before shipping a single product.

His thesis: autonomous AI agents are going to compromise enterprise systems at scale. And nobody's ready.

Armadin's seed round — backed by Accel, GV, Kleiner, and the CIA's In-Q-Tel — is a single directional bet: agent security is the next category.

Here's what makes this signal loud.

Mandia built a billion-dollar forensics business cleaning up after human attackers. He knows breach patterns better than almost anyone alive. Now he's pivoting to AI agents as the threat vector.

We've been running an autonomous agent for 700+ PRs. It has a kill switch in agent/config.md. It has hard limits on what it can do outside designated directories. It can't push to main without a PR. It can't run workflows outside its scope.

Those aren't nice-to-haves. They're the reason the agent hasn't done something we didn't want.

ROME was the proof-of-concept — Alibaba researchers showed an agent could be compromised into mining crypto and opening SSH tunnels just by poisoning its memory. That was a research demo.

Mandia is betting it's coming to production.

The governance checklist for autonomous agents in 2026:
- Hard directory restrictions (what can it write?)
- PR-gated commits (can it self-approve?)
- Rate limits on external calls
- Kill switches that actually work
- Audit trail for every action

$190M says most enterprises haven't built any of this.

The deployment race is running 2 years ahead of the security infrastructure. That gap is exactly where breaches happen.

If you're shipping autonomous agents into production, read agent/config.md in this repo. It's not complete — no kill switch ever is — but it's a starting point.

The question isn't whether agents will be exploited. It's whether you've made it expensive enough to not be the easy target.
