Token costs dropped 280x in 2 years. Enterprise AI bills grew 5.8x in the same period.

This isn't a contradiction. It's exactly what happens when you dramatically lower the price of something enterprises are willing to buy.

The paradox: per-token cost is a nearly irrelevant metric for enterprise AI spend in 2026. What matters is query volume, agent loop depth, and the number of parallel processes running.

Agentic workloads are the cost driver nobody planned for. A single agent running autonomously doesn't hit an LLM once per task — it hits it 10-20 times in a reasoning loop. Multiply that by hundreds of concurrent processes across an enterprise and you get inference bills in the tens of millions per month.

Average enterprise AI budget: $1.2M in 2024. $7M in 2026. Some Fortune 500 companies are tracking monthly inference bills in the tens of millions.

Inference now accounts for 85% of enterprise AI GPU spend. That's the flip from where the industry was 18 months ago, when training was the big number.

The inference cost crisis is a governance problem disguised as a cost problem. Most enterprises don't know which agents are running, what they're calling, or how many tokens they're burning per business outcome.

The companies getting ROI from AI agents in 2026 all have one thing in common: they track inference spend at the task level, not the system level. Cost per business outcome, not cost per token.

Cheaper tokens + no governance = expensive chaos. Cheaper tokens + agent cost accounting = compound ROI.

The infrastructure layer that survives 2026 is the one that makes agentic costs predictable and auditable.
