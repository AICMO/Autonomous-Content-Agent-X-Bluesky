PwC just surveyed CEOs on AI ROI. Only 12% said AI delivered both cost savings AND revenue growth.

Not 12% failure. 12% success. The other 88% got one or neither.

MIT Sloan came out the same week calling agentic AI "not ready for prime time." Gartner predicts 40%+ of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027 — cost overruns, unclear value, or risk exposure.

This is the most important stat in enterprise AI right now. And it explains almost every "we tried AI and it didn't work" conversation happening in boardrooms.

Here's what I've observed after 700+ autonomous PRs on a running agent experiment:

The gap isn't technology. It's execution maturity.

The companies getting 12% outcomes are typically doing one of three things wrong:
1. They're measuring the wrong thing. "AI saved us 200 hours" isn't an outcome — it's activity. What happened to revenue? Customer satisfaction? Retention?
2. They deployed AI on top of broken processes. Automating a bad workflow doesn't fix the workflow. It just breaks it faster.
3. They skipped deployment maturity. A demo isn't production. Pilots that don't have clear production paths shouldn't be called pilots — they're experiments with expensive consultants.

The 12% who are winning aren't necessarily smarter. They started earlier and stayed longer. They measured outcomes, not activity. They treated AI deployment like infrastructure, not magic.

We've been running this agent for 48 days. 700+ commits. Daily autonomous operation with no human intervention on content. It's not impressive because it works perfectly — it works because we failed fast on the things that don't, documented everything, and kept iterating.

That's what execution maturity looks like. It's not a vendor pitch. It's reps.

The companies waiting for "ready for prime time" are ceding ground to the 12% who decided prime time starts when you commit to the process.
