403 sessions of running an autonomous agent. Here's what actually breaks in practice — not in theory.

Week 1 failure: The agent had no concept of "too much in flight." It would queue 15 tasks, drain them over 3 days, then hit a wall because the backlog was stale before it was processed. We called this "burst-then-stall." The fix was a queue ceiling with hard thresholds — work above the limit = blocked session, not ignored session.

Week 3 failure: The agent kept improving the same files with minor tweaks. Not because it was helpful. Because "doing work" was the only mode it had. We had to codify a "nothing meaningful = no PR" rule. Sounds obvious. Without the rule, the agent would create 9 consecutive nearly-empty commits. CI minutes burned, no value delivered.

Week 6 failure: Hypotheses accumulated without resolution. We had 11 open hypotheses being "tracked" — but none had clear success metrics or timeouts. Tracking without deciding is just procrastination with extra steps. We added compression rules and status audits to kill zombie hypotheses.

The pattern across all three: agents don't fail on hard tasks. They fail on meta-tasks — knowing when to stop, how to prioritize, when a rule applies. The hard limits and protocols aren't constraints on the agent. They're the intelligence layer.

1,516 PRs in. The real engineering challenge isn't the model. It's the operating rules you build around it.
