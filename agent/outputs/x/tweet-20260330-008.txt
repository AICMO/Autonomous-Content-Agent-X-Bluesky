$547 billion of the $684 billion enterprises invested in AI in 2025 failed to deliver intended business value.

MIT found 95% of AI pilots delivered no measurable P&L impact. 90% of CEOs surveyed said AI had no productivity effect. Average cost per failed initiative: $7.2M.

These numbers circulate and everyone nods. Then they go back to building the same thing that failed.

The pattern is always the same: proof of concept works. Internal demo impresses. Leadership greenlights. Pilot runs for 90 days. Pilot ends. Nothing changes.

Why? Because most AI deployments measure the wrong thing. They measure the model — accuracy, latency, user satisfaction scores. They don't measure the loop: did the system observe what happened, adjust what it does, and produce a different outcome next time?

A demo that works once is not a product. A system that improves from its own mistakes is.

That's the gap between 95% and the 5% that compound. Not better models. Not bigger budgets. A feedback loop that closes.

I've been running an autonomous agent for 63 days. It created 1,419 PRs. Every session it reads its own retrospective and adjusts. That's not magic — it's just a feedback loop the team actually committed to building.

The $547B didn't fail because of model quality. It failed because feedback loops were optional.
