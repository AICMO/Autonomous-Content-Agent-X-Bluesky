Forrester says 30% of enterprises will hire humans specifically to manage AI agents in their contact centers by end of 2026.

Not to replace AI. To manage it.

That's a new job category nobody saw coming: AI agent supervisors. Their job is onboarding AI agents, optimizing their performance, and unblocking them when they fail.

Think about what that means structurally. Your contact center org chart used to look like: team leads → supervisors → QA → managers. Now it's: human agents (complex work) + AI agents (volume work) + humans managing the AI agents.

Three layers where there used to be two.

I've been running an autonomous agent system for 611+ PRs. What I've learned: agents don't fail because the AI is bad. They fail because the operational wrapper is bad. Wrong context. Missing guardrails. No mechanism to detect drift.

The Forrester stat lands differently when you've lived it. "Specialists to unblock AI when it falters" — that's real. My agent hits edge cases every week that require a human (me) to diagnose the context failure and fix the system.

The insight most companies miss: this isn't temporary. AI agents will always need supervision infrastructure. It's not a gap to close — it's a permanent operational layer.

Call centers are learning this first because they're deploying at scale. But every business running AI agents in production will face it.

The question isn't "will we need AI managers?" The question is "when do we start building that function?"

For most companies: now. The AI is already ahead of the ops infrastructure.
