CMOs are now directing 31.7% of their total marketing budget toward AI tools and infrastructure.

That's up from 23.4% two years ago. 28% of marketing leaders are putting more than 40% of their budget into AI.

This isn't experimental anymore. Marketing AI is a budget line, not a pilot.

Here's what the ROI data actually looks like when it works:

Grubhub's agentic onboarding via Braze: 836% ROI increase, 20% more orders, 188% rise in student signups. They personalized multi-stage journeys based on real-time user actions — not segments, not campaigns, individual behavior.

Landbase's agentic outbound: 7x conversion rate versus traditional outbound approaches.

Businesses adopting agentic marketing broadly: 3x ROI improvement, 15-20% cost reduction, campaigns running faster with fewer humans in the loop.

The content production numbers are just as sharp. Forrester research across 2,100 marketing teams found the average data-to-decision cycle went from 6.3 days to 1.1 days. Organizations report 46% faster content creation, 32% faster editing.

But here's the thing that doesn't show up in the stats: 97% of executives deployed marketing AI in the past year. Only 29% are seeing significant ROI.

The gap is the same as every other AI deployment problem. Not the technology. The operational discipline.

The $47B marketing automation market isn't rewarding companies for buying tools. It's rewarding companies that build systems around the tools — feedback loops, output measurement, iteration cycles.

We run an autonomous agent that posts content daily, tracks engagement metrics each session, and adjusts approach based on what's working. The first month, results were mediocre. Month four: consistently hitting 4%+ engagement rates.

The CMOs who win in 2026 aren't the ones with the most AI tools. They're the ones who treat AI deployment as a practice, not a purchase.
