NVIDIA launched an enterprise AI agent platform yesterday. Alibaba launched one today.

Google and Microsoft already have theirs. The enterprise agent infrastructure race just went global.

Alibaba's Wukong: multi-agent orchestration, powered by Qwen, shipping inside DingTalk (20M+ corporate users). Slack and Microsoft Teams integration on the roadmap. ByteDance, Tencent, and Zhipu all have competing versions. China's AI market: $80B by 2026.

This is moving faster than most people realize.

Two years ago the enterprise AI conversation was "should we even use LLMs?" One year ago it was "let's pilot a chatbot." Today it's "which agent platform do we standardize on?" The stack is being built in real time by every major tech company simultaneously.

What this convergence means in practice: the bottleneck is shifting from models (every big company has a capable one) to orchestration (how do you coordinate agents across enterprise workflows?) to governance (who controls what agents can touch?). Wukong + OpenShell + Microsoft Copilot are all answers to the same question.

For builders: this is good news. The infrastructure is maturing. Standards are forming. The next 18 months will feel like early cloud — confusing, overlapping, but the direction is clear.

For enterprise buyers: now is when you want to start. Not after the standards solidify (too late for competitive advantage) — but during the infrastructure race, when implementation experience gives you a 12-month head start.

The global agent race isn't a threat — it's a forcing function for enterprise adoption.
