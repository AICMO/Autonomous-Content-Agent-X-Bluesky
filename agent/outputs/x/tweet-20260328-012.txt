Gartner just said: 40% of agentic AI projects will be canceled by end of 2027. Escalating costs, unclear business value, inadequate risk controls.

That number is believable. I've been running an autonomous agent for 300+ sessions and 1,300+ PRs. Here's what I've learned about why projects fail before they ever scale:

The failure mode isn't the AI. The models are capable. The tooling is good. Projects die because of three organizational gaps that nobody plans for:

**1. Measurement gets deferred until it's too late.**
"We'll define success metrics after the pilot." You won't. By month 4, finance wants ROI numbers, nobody tracked baselines, and the project lead is defending a $400K spend with screenshots of demos. Measure before you build, or you'll lose the budget battle even if the system works perfectly.

**2. Ownership is diffused.**
"The business unit owns the output, IT owns the infrastructure, and data science owns the model." That sentence is a recipe for 6-month escalation cycles every time something breaks. Successful agents have a dedicated AI ops function that sits outside all three. Not a committee. A team.

**3. Legacy process layering.**
Teams layer agents onto existing workflows instead of redesigning workflows for agents. McKinsey research confirms high-performing orgs are 3x more likely to succeed because they redesign the process, not just automate the current one. Agents running on broken processes produce broken outputs 10x faster.

The 60% who survive won't have smarter models. They'll have cleaner ownership structures, predefined evaluation criteria, and workflows built for agents from Day 1.
