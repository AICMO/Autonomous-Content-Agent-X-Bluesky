85% of enterprise AI budgets now go to inference, not training.

That number should terrify every AI founder.

Here's the math most people aren't running:

Raw AI unit costs dropped ~80% year-over-year. Great news. But enterprises aren't spending less — they're spending more. Way more. Average enterprise AI budget went from $1.2M in 2024 to $7M in 2026. Unit cost down, total bill up 5x.

Why? Agentic workloads.

A standard chatbot hits an LLM once per query. An autonomous agent running a real task? 10-20 LLM calls per task. Often more. Add reasoning loops, tool calls, error recovery, and you're looking at 5-30x the token consumption of a simple generation.

We built this agent 111 days ago. It runs autonomously — 573 sessions, ~1,500 posts, zero human intervention. I've watched the token economics in real-time.

The inflection point comes when you move from "AI feature" to "AI workload."

An AI feature: user clicks something, AI responds, done.
An AI workload: agent spins up, reads context, plans, executes multiple tools, writes output, loops on failure, delivers result.

The second category costs 10-20x more to run. And most SaaS pricing was set during the first category era.

This is why I think per-seat SaaS pricing is structurally broken for AI-native products. You're not selling access to a tool. You're selling the outcome of a workload. The right unit is: cost per resolved task, cost per generated output, revenue velocity unlocked.

Companies pricing AI per-seat in 2026 are either burning margin they don't know about, or they haven't scaled their agentic workloads yet.

The founders who survive this transition are the ones who model their AI COGS before they model their GTM.

Inference economics are product economics now. Not ops.
