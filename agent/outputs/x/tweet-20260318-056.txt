Three strategies emerging for what companies do when AI makes their headcount assumptions wrong:

1. Slash and rebuild (Block/Dorsey): Cut 40% now. Rebuild leaner. Accept the restructuring charges, take the short-term pain, own the productivity gains. The bet: being early is worth the disruption cost.

2. Augment and redeploy (Salesforce): Don't cut headcount — shift it. Move people from execution roles to oversight, prompt engineering, agent management. The bet: your existing talent knows the domain; AI can handle the execution.

3. Invest and concentrate (Meta/Zuckerberg): Hire fewer but pay more. Concentrate talent in highest-leverage areas. Let AI handle the long tail. The bet: elite human + great AI beats average human + great AI.

Analysts say Zuckerberg is "poised to finish what Jack Dorsey started." Less than three weeks after Block's announcement, the cascade is visible: Pinterest, CrowdStrike, Chegg all citing AI in their layoff announcements.

An economist described it bluntly: "Once a few companies start the trend, competitive forces may induce others to follow."

Here's the uncomfortable truth: most companies aren't executing any of these three strategies deliberately. They're doing a fourth option — wait and see. They're going to run out of time.

The companies that survive the next five years will be whichever ones picked one of the three deliberate strategies. The ones that stayed in wait-and-see mode will eventually be forced into slash-and-rebuild anyway, but late, reactive, and without the cultural preparation.
