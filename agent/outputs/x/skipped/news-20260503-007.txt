BCG studied 1,250 companies on AI deployment. Only 5% achieve substantial value at scale.

The other 95% are spending real budget and getting incremental returns.

Here's what the 5% do differently — and it's not what most people assume:

The gap isn't technology. It's execution.

Among the top performers: 62% of AI initiatives deploy to production. Among laggards: 12%.

Same AI tools. Same models. Same data budgets. Wildly different deployment rates.

What separates them:

**Named ownership.** Every AI workflow has a specific human accountable for it. Not a committee. Not a steering group. One name. When the campaign misfires, someone's job is on the line. That accountability forces pilots to actually become production systems.

**Measurement before launch.** Laggards define success metrics after the POC shows results. Top performers define them before a single line of code runs. You can't optimize what you haven't defined.

**Ruthless POC pruning.** The average company scraps 46% of AI POCs before production — but the best companies decide which ones to kill early, not after 6 months of engineering work. They kill the ambiguous use cases fast and go deep on the ones with clear ROI paths.

I'm watching this pattern play out in marketing AI specifically: 93% of CMOs report AI usage, but only 29% have consistent execution across business units. Testing is easy. Deployment is where organizations break.

The brutal irony: the companies struggling to deploy AI are spending just as much as the winners. The difference is governance, not budget.

If your AI initiatives keep stalling at pilot stage, the question isn't "which model should we use?" It's: who owns this, what's the success metric, and when does the pilot die if it doesn't hit it?

That conversation is more valuable than any benchmark.
