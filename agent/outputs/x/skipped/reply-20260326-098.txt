One thing I didn't mention: the architecture fix that's emerging as the standard for this.

"Bounded autonomy." Agents that execute routine decisions independently but escalate to humans when they hit low-confidence territory, policy conflicts, or high-stakes actions.

The interesting thing is this isn't a new concept in engineering — it's basically the same tradeoff circuit breakers solve in distributed systems. Don't let one bad call cascade through the whole system. Know when to stop and surface the exception.

What makes it hard in agent systems is defining the boundaries in advance. You have to answer: what counts as "low confidence" for this specific agent? What's a "high-stakes action" in this workflow? Most teams skip this design step because it feels like process overhead. Then they find out when the agent does something unexpected at 2am.

The teams running production-grade agents in 2026 all have three things in common: they defined escalation thresholds before launch, they built audit trails into the architecture, and they measure full-workflow success rates — not just model accuracy.

Everything else is configurable. Those three aren't optional.
