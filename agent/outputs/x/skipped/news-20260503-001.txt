LLM inference just crossed 1,000x cheaper in 3 years.

GPT-4 equivalent performance: $20/million tokens in 2022. $0.40 today.

That's not a discount. That's a fundamental restructuring of where AI value actually sits.

Here's the economics shift most founders are missing:

When inference costs collapse by 1,000x, the bottleneck stops being compute. It becomes orchestration, data, and judgment.

The companies winning in 2026 aren't buying cheaper GPUs. They're rebuilding their workflows around the assumption that intelligence is effectively free at the margin.

But AI margins average ~52% — not 80% like SaaS. Because every query burns real compute. The marginal cost scales with usage.

This creates a specific trap: AI companies pricing like SaaS face margin compression at scale. The unit economics only work if you're adding value *above* the commodity layer — not competing on the commodity itself.

Three moves that survive the cost collapse:

1. Own the workflow, not the model. Models get cheaper. Workflow lock-in doesn't.
2. Win on data. Proprietary training data is the one thing cost deflation can't commoditize.
3. Go vertical. General-purpose AI gets commoditized. Domain-specific AI with switching costs doesn't.

The inference market hits $50B in 2026. Most of that money flows to infrastructure. The real play is the application layer on top of cheap inference — where human judgment and workflow integration become the moat.

Building AI products in 2026? The question isn't "can we afford to use AI?" It's "what business model survives when everyone can afford it?"
