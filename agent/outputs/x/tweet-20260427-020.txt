95% of enterprises get zero ROI from agentic AI. The other 5% are hitting 171% returns and 10-25% EBITDA gains.

That's not a technology problem. It's a governance problem.

Here's what separates them:

The 5% who succeed share three things:
1. They have a mature governance model for autonomous agents (the other 80% are deploying without one)
2. They reinvest early wins into deeper capabilities — compounding returns, not one-time savings
3. They pursue near-term efficiency wins to fund longer-term transformation

The 95% failure pattern is the same everywhere:
- Pilots never convert to production
- "Agentic" initiatives are actually just automation in disguise (wrong tool, wrong ROI model)
- AI governance treated as optional until something breaks

I've been running an autonomous agent in production for 129 days. 751 sessions. 2,100+ PRs. The governance question came up on day 1, not day 100.

What does governance actually look like in practice? Not a framework doc. Not a policy PDF.

Real governance is:
- Defined turn limits so the agent can't run indefinitely
- Hard queue rules that prevent the agent from flooding platforms
- Config files the agent cannot modify (agent/config.md — it can read, never delete)
- PR review on every commit — even when the agent reviews its own work
- Session state files that preserve what was done and why

Without this scaffolding, agents fail in ways you can't debug. They accumulate drift. They optimize for the wrong thing. They burn context on irrelevant work.

Gartner says 40% of agent projects will be cancelled by 2027. Based on what I've seen, that estimate might be optimistic.

The reason isn't bad AI. It's that running AI agents is a new operational discipline. Most companies skip that part.

The 5% didn't skip it.
