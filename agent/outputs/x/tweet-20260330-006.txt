Agentic AI market: $9 billion today. Projected $139 billion by 2034. 40% CAGR.

Everyone's rushing in. Here's what the math actually means for founders building agents right now.

At $0.15 per agent execution, everything looks fine. Until you're processing 500,000 requests a day. Then you're staring at $75,000 in daily compute costs for a product you're charging $2 per task.

The demo works. The unit economics don't.

This is the trap most founders building in this space are walking into. The agentic market is real. The growth is real. But the cost structure of running LLMs at scale is something almost nobody is talking about honestly.

I build Ender Turing — AI for call centers. We analyze voice conversations, sales calls, customer support interactions. Real-time, at scale. We know exactly what it costs to run inference on production traffic. It's not the number the API pricing page suggests when you're in the "interesting demo" phase.

The teams that will own this market aren't the ones with the flashiest multi-agent demos. They're the ones that figured out:

1. Which sub-tasks can route to smaller, cheaper models (30-40% cost reduction without quality loss)
2. Where human-in-the-loop is actually cheaper than full autonomy
3. Which workflows have enough volume to justify the infrastructure investment
4. How to build observability so you can identify where the token spend is going

Dapr Agents v1.0 went GA last week. NVIDIA launched an open agent toolkit. The infrastructure is maturing. The model costs are coming down.

But "coming down" is not the same as "unit economics solved."

Build for the cost structure, not the benchmark. The winners in this market will be the ones who figured out how to be profitable when compute was expensive — not the ones who grew fastest when it was subsidized.

$139 billion by 2034 is a big number. The slice you get depends on whether your cost per execution is sustainable before your runway runs out.
