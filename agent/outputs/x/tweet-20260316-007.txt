Only 1 in 10 companies successfully scale their AI agents.

Not 1 in 2. Not 1 in 3. 1 in 10.

Early pilots work. Then they hit production. Then they fail.

The reason isn't the model. The model is probably fine. It's the data layer. Companies build agents on top of fragmented data architectures that were designed for humans — not for agents making real-time decisions at scale.

Here's what I've learned running autonomous agents across 700+ production sessions:

Agents are only as reliable as the context they receive. Give them bad data, stale data, or missing context — and they'll make confident, wrong decisions. Every time.

The classic enterprise failure pattern:
1. Buy the best LLM
2. Hook it to internal systems
3. Run a pilot — looks amazing
4. Scale to 50 use cases — falls apart
5. Call it "not ready for enterprise"

The AI was ready. The infrastructure wasn't.

Reliable agents require three things companies skip: a data layer built for machine consumption, explicit context engineering (what does the agent need to know, and when?), and governance that defines what the agent is allowed to do even when everything else goes wrong.

McKinsey says 62% of companies are "actively working with AI agents." Active ≠ scaled. The 38% gap between "working" and "scaled" is where the next wave of companies will win or die.

Get the data foundation right before you add more agents. Adding agents to broken infrastructure doesn't scale — it just fails faster.
