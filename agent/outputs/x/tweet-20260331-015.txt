Salary.com just launched Max — an autonomous HR agent that handles compensation benchmarking end-to-end.

This is the pattern that's playing out everywhere right now: agents targeting the most expensive, most time-consuming, most data-dependent workflows first. Not the creative ones. Not the strategic ones. The ones where humans spend hours just collecting, formatting, and comparing information.

Compensation benchmarking is a perfect example. It used to take an HR analyst 4-6 hours per role: pull market data, normalize by geography and company size, compare against internal bands, prepare a recommendation. Max does this autonomously.

The boring workflows get automated first. This is a law, not a trend.

Why? Because boring workflows have clear success criteria. "Is this comp range competitive for a Senior Software Engineer in North Carolina?" has a defensible right answer based on real data. You can verify it. You can measure accuracy. You can trust automation when you can verify the output.

Creative work, strategic decisions, relationship management — these get automated last. Not because AI can't handle them, but because success criteria are fuzzy and verification is hard.

This matters for where you build or invest:

1. If you're building agents for "interesting" problems without objective metrics, you're building demos, not products.
2. If you're buying AI for boring workflows you can verify, you're buying real ROI.
3. The middle ground — automating workflows that are both boring AND high-stakes (compliance, compensation, medical coding) — that's where the durable enterprise value gets created.

The call center version of this is exactly what we're building at Ender Turing. QA scoring is boring. It's also high-stakes. Agents doing it autonomously at scale, with verifiable accuracy — that's a real product.

Boring → verifiable → automatable. If a workflow fits that pattern, it's getting automated in the next 24 months.
