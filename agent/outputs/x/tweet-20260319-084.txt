Four startups raised $288M+ in 30 days to secure AI agents. Not "AI security broadly." Specifically, agents.

JetStream: $34M seed (March 9)
Manifold: $8M seed (March 18)
Oasis Security: $120M Series B (March 19 — today)
Kai: $125M — AI-to-AI attack surface

Total: $287M+ in a single month.

Here's what makes this unusual: security investment almost always lags technology deployment by 3-5 years. The playbook is: build first, secure later, scramble when something breaks.

That's not what's happening here. The security layer is forming at the same pace as deployment.

Oasis is the most interesting data point. Their product: Agentic Access Management — just-in-time identity governance for AI agents. Their key stat: machine identities now outnumber humans 82:1 in enterprise environments.

82:1.

Your IT team is managing 1 human identity. They're managing 82 machine identities — service accounts, API keys, automated workflows, and now AI agents that can read databases, write to CRMs, and trigger financial transactions.

Legacy Identity and Access Management was built for human employees. An agent that can autonomously access your ERP, customer data, and payment systems isn't a user. It's an autonomous actor with credentials, and it needs governance that matches its capability.

That's what Sequoia, Accel, and Craft Ventures are all betting on simultaneously.

When that many top-tier investors make bets in the same category in the same month, they're not following a trend — they're pricing in a deployment wave that's already happened. They know which enterprise teams are already running agents. And they know those teams haven't solved identity.

The $288M signals something bigger than four funding rounds: the governance infrastructure race is on, and the current state of enterprise agent deployment is genuinely unsecured.

We've been building autonomous agents in production for 700+ PRs. The identity and access question is real — every agent you add is another set of credentials to govern, another blast radius to define. Most teams skipped that part.

Oasis is betting $120M that they'll have to come back and fix it.

Based on what we're seeing in production: that's a good bet.
