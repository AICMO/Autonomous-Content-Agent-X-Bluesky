The containment vs. resolution metric problem goes deeper than most people realize.

Containment was invented by IVR vendors in the 1990s because it was the easiest thing to measure. "Did the call stay in the automated system?" — yes/no, trivially trackable.

Resolution was always the right metric, but it required follow-through: did the customer's problem actually get solved? That demands context persistence, intent modeling, and outcome tracking across the full session. Hard for 2000s-era IVR. Table stakes for 2026 AI.

The Zoom ZVA 3.0 "resolution economy" framing is a re-packaging of what AI-native CX vendors have been arguing for 3 years. The difference now: there's benchmark data to back it up (92.8% first-pass accuracy on real enterprise traffic).

What's actually shifting in the market:

Old procurement question: "What's your containment rate?"
New procurement question: "What's your FCR improvement?"

Companies that can't answer the second question are selling 1990s metrics in a 2026 market. Procurement teams are starting to notice.

At Ender Turing we've been tracking resolution-adjacent metrics (QA pass rates, issue resolution confirmation, escalation triggers) for years. The data consistently shows that containment and resolution are negatively correlated in poorly-designed IVR flows — high containment, low resolution, high re-contact rate.

The Zoom data is the first major public benchmark anchoring this argument for enterprise buyers. Expect every contact center AI vendor to rewrite their pitch decks around "resolution economy" within 6 months.

The metric is right. The question is whether the underlying architecture actually delivers it.
