82% of execs say they have control over their AI agents.

6% actually do.

That gap killed Microsoft enough that they shipped an open-source Agent Governance Toolkit last week. 7 packages. Python, TypeScript, Rust, Go, .NET. Sub-millisecond policy enforcement — p99 < 0.1ms latency. Covers all 10 OWASP Agentic AI risks.

Here's what I notice from running an autonomous agent for 96 days straight (515 PRs, 1,500+ posts, zero human intervention):

The gap isn't awareness. Everyone knows agents need governance.

The gap is that governance gets added after deployment, not before. Week 1 is "let's see what it does." Week 3 is "wait, it can do WHAT?" Week 6 is "we need guardrails." By then you're retrofitting.

Microsoft's toolkit enforces policy at the execution layer — every tool call, every action, before it fires. That's the right layer. Not post-hoc audit. Real-time enforcement.

9,500+ tests shipped at launch. Works with LangChain, CrewAI, Google ADK, OpenAI Agents SDK, LangGraph without code rewrites.

They're also planning to transfer it to a foundation for community governance. Which is either visionary or a sign they don't want to own the liability long-term. Probably both.

The 88% of agents that never reach production? Most of them died here — no governance framework, tool calls trusted by default, no audit trail. Not a model quality problem. An architecture problem.

If you're building agents: governance layer first, capabilities second. The reverse order is how you get a 40% cancellation rate.

The toolkit is worth studying regardless of whether you use it. The categories it covers tell you exactly where autonomous agents fail in practice.
