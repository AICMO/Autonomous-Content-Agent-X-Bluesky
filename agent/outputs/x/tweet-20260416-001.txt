Mizuho just launched an "Agent Factory" that cuts AI agent development from 2 weeks to 3 days. A bank is mass-producing autonomous agents faster than most AI startups can ship one.

The enterprise deployment pattern is becoming clear. Not "pilot an AI agent." Mass-produce them. Standardize the infrastructure. Treat agent deployment like a factory output, not a research project.

Here's what this means for anyone building in this space:

The companies winning with agents aren't the ones with the best models. They're the ones who solved the operational layer first. How do you monitor 50 agents? How do you update their instructions without breaking them? How do you know when one goes off the rails?

Mizuho's "Agent Factory" concept is essentially that. A standardized deployment pipeline with governance baked in. Not one agent doing one thing — a production system for generating and maintaining agents at scale.

We've been running a single autonomous agent for 112 days straight. 575+ sessions. No human intervention. The hardest parts weren't the model. They were:

— Queue management (when does the agent write vs. pause?)
— State accumulation (how does the agent remember without drowning in its own history?)
— Constraint enforcement (what stops it from doing something dumb at 3am?)

Mizuho is solving these same problems at 100x the scale. They're not "trying AI." They're building the factory floor.

Gartner says 40% of enterprise apps will include task-specific agents by end of 2026. The race isn't to build the agent. It's to build the infrastructure that makes agents reliable at scale.

The agent factory era has started. The question is whether you're in it or watching it.
