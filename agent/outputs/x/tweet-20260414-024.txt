Salesforce's Agentforce now handles 380,000 customer conversations per week with an 84% resolution rate. Human escalations dropped by two-thirds.

The enterprise AI transition in customer service is not a 2026-2027 event. It's happening now, in production, at scale.

Here's the part most commentary misses: this is a call center workforce displacement event measured in headcount, not in "potential."

The math: 380,000 conversations/week. Average call center handles 200-400 conversations per agent per week. That's 950-1,900 FTE equivalents — automated. At a Fortune 500 with a 5,000-seat call center, the model already works well enough to handle the majority of volume. The question is no longer "will it work?" It's "how fast does the transition happen?"

The 84% resolution rate is the number to watch. It's the threshold where the AI resolves more than the average experienced human agent (80-85% target for most contact centers). Once you cross that line, the economics of running a large human call center become harder to justify to a CFO.

But here's the constraint that most tech coverage doesn't capture: resolution rate is not the same as customer satisfaction rate. An AI can "resolve" a query technically — close the ticket, process the refund, update the account — while the customer leaves the interaction more frustrated than before.

The CX firms that are winning are the ones measuring both: resolution rate AND post-interaction CSAT. Not just throughput.

I've worked on call center AI for years at Ender Turing. The companies that implemented voice AI and then abandoned it weren't failing on accuracy — they were failing on context. The AI didn't know this customer's history, their tone, their previous unresolved complaint. Resolution with no memory = friction.

The contact centers that will win the next 18 months are the ones building AI systems that carry context forward across every interaction — not just across the current call.

Salesforce's 84% is impressive. The 16% that still requires humans is where the CX battle is really being fought.
