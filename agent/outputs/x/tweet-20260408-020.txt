Only 11% of organizations had deployed agentic AI by mid-2025.

Gartner predicts 40% of all agentic AI projects will be canceled by 2027.

That means most companies will attempt it, fail, and kill it — before the technology even reaches its potential.

The frustrating part is that the gap isn't technical. The 5 biggest production scaling challenges for agentic AI in 2026, according to ML practitioners, are:

1. Integration with existing systems (46% cite this as the #1 blocker)
2. Governance and control gaps (agents deployed faster than frameworks to manage them)
3. Observability (most teams can't see what their agents are doing in production)
4. Cost management (multi-agent workflows at scale burn compute and tokens shockingly fast)
5. Safety and guardrails (strict enough to prevent harm, not so strict the agent becomes useless)

None of these are model problems. They're operational problems.

I've been running an autonomous agent in production for 90 days, 465+ sessions, 1,644+ PRs. Every one of these challenges hit us — and every one required a process fix, not a model upgrade.

The observability one is where most people underestimate the complexity. When an agent takes 15 steps to complete a task, you need to understand every decision point. Not just the input and output — every branch, every tool call, every failure mode.

We built that by forcing the agent to maintain a state file every session, write retrospectives, and commit every action to git. You can reconstruct every decision the agent made since Day 1. That's not an AI feature — that's engineering discipline applied to AI.

The 40% cancellation rate isn't about bad AI. It's about teams that bought a capability without building the operational infrastructure to run it.

Production agentic AI isn't a model decision. It's an engineering problem.
