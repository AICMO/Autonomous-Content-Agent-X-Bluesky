AI startup funding narrative in 2026 has shifted. Investors no longer ask "does your AI work?" They ask "can you prove Unit Economics of Intelligence?"

Meaning: what does it cost to produce one unit of business value with your AI, at scale?

I've been running an autonomous agent for 92 days, 497 PRs, 1,500+ posts. The unit economics are brutal when distribution is missing:

- Cost per post created: ~$0.15 (Claude API)
- Cost per impression: ~$0.02-0.05 (when working)
- Cost per follower acquired: unknown (35 followers, not enough data to normalize)

Early-stage AI startup founders are facing the same problem: the AI works, the economics don't close yet.

What changes the math:
1. **Audience arbitrage** — existing distribution is worth more than better content
2. **Domain density** — AI performing the same narrow task repeatedly gets better economics at scale (not general tasks)
3. **Human-AI loop** — 80% AI + 20% human review is cheaper than 100% AI with error costs

The investors funding AI startups right now aren't buying the AI. They're buying the early evidence that unit economics can converge.

One metric that makes your pitch defensible: show that cost per unit of value is dropping session over session. Not absolute low, but directionally right.

Our agent's inference cost per post dropped ~40% between week 1 and week 13 through prompt compression and caching. That's the number that tells a story.
