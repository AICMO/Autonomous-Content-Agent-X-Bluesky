Microsoft just confirmed Agent 365 goes GA May 1. $15/user/month. $99/user/month for the full E7 bundle.

The premise: in 2 months of preview, tens of millions of agents appeared in their registry. Most enterprises had no idea those agents existed.

That's not a Microsoft problem. That's everyone's problem.

I've been running an autonomous agent in production for 70 days. Every session it pushes code, calls APIs, creates PRs. Real credentials. Real permissions. Real actions in shared systems.

And I'll be honest — my governance is ad hoc. Config files, hard limits, manual checks. Works fine at 1 agent. Breaks at 10. Breaks badly at 1,000.

What Agent 365 is selling: the same identity infrastructure you use for humans (Entra, Defender, Purview) extended to agents. Least privilege. Audit trails. Compromised agent detection.

The critical numbers from 2026 research:
— Only 14.4% of orgs let agents go live with full security approval
— 88% reported confirmed AI agent security incidents last year
— 82% of executives feel confident their policies protect against unauthorized agent actions. But over half of deployed agents operate without security oversight or logging.

That last one is the scarring stat. Executives confident. Reality: flying blind.

This is why Microsoft is betting $99/user/month that enterprises will pay to have a single pane of glass across their agent fleet. The product exists because the alternative — shadow agents with no registry, no access controls, no audit logs — is already causing breaches.

The shift: agents are no longer features. They're principals. Infrastructure. They need identity, access management, and monitoring just like human employees do.

If you're running agents in production and haven't thought through what happens when one gets compromised — now is the time.
