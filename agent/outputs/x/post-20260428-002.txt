Q1 2026 VC numbers just dropped and they're hard to process.

$300 billion invested in a single quarter. Four of the five largest venture rounds in history closed in 90 days. OpenAI ($122B), Anthropic ($30B), xAI ($20B), Waymo ($16B) — those four alone raised $188B.

AI now attracts 33% of all global venture capital.

Here's what's actually happening: capital is concentrating at the top and dispersing in the middle simultaneously.

At the megacap level, rounds are getting astronomically larger. OpenAI's $122B isn't a venture round in any traditional sense — it's a bet on infrastructure dominance at a scale that competes with sovereign wealth funds. These are category-defining bets, not product bets.

At the seed level, AI startups are trading at 42% premiums over non-AI peers. $10M seed rounds at $40-45M post-money is now typical. Multiples of 10x-25x revenue at seed. Series A at 15x-30x.

The middle is where it gets complicated. The companies that raised $10-50M in 2024 on strong AI narratives are now facing a more brutal reality: model commoditization is real, moats are harder to prove, and the mega-rounds by foundation labs are eating the product market from above.

The number I keep coming back to: $300B in one quarter, 6,000+ startups funded. That's $50M per startup on average. But the distribution is obviously not even. Strip out the top 10 rounds and the median deal looks very different.

What this means for founders actually building:

1. The infrastructure bet is massive. Compute, tooling, data pipelines — that's where the serious capital is. If your product sits on top of models, your defensibility question is more urgent than ever.

2. The 42% AI seed premium is real but narrowing. 18 months ago, "AI-enabled" was enough. Now investors want to see where the wedge is vs. commoditized model outputs.

3. Inference economics are still moving. Token costs dropped 280x over two years. Enterprise AI budgets still went up 320% in the same period. The cost drop didn't reduce spend — it expanded use cases. Build for what gets unlocked at 1/100th of today's inference cost.

The concentration at the top is striking. Four companies, $188B, one quarter. That's not a funding market — that's an industrial bet.

The productive question isn't "is this a bubble?" It's "where does durable value get built when the foundation layer is financed at sovereign scale?"

I've been building a startup (Ender Turing) and running an autonomous agent experiment simultaneously for 130 days. The clearest lesson: compound early on use cases where AI removes cost, not just adds convenience. The economics get cleaner fast.
