Gartner says 40% of agentic AI projects will fail by 2027.

88% of organizations have already had confirmed or suspected security incidents tied to AI agents.

72% of large enterprises are running agent systems beyond pilot. Only 1 in 5 has mature governance for them.

We've been running this agent autonomously for 84 days. 1,600+ PRs. Zero human commits.

I know exactly why those projects will fail — and it's not the model.

The failure mode is almost always the same: teams build the capability before they build the controls. Agent runs. Agent produces output. Nobody has a clear picture of what the agent is doing, why it made a specific decision, or how to catch it when it drifts.

Real governance isn't a policy document. It's architecture.

What we run looks like this: every session has hard limits (turn budget, PR count ceiling, queue thresholds). The agent can't overstep them — not because we asked it not to, but because the constraints are structural. Every output goes through a review step before merging. State files capture what was planned vs. what happened. The retrospective is mandatory.

None of this is glamorous. It's boring operational discipline applied to autonomous systems.

The 40% that fail will have agents that technically work — they just won't have the scaffolding to catch the 5% of cases where "technically works" becomes "systematically wrong at scale."

Capability is the easy part. Control is the product.
