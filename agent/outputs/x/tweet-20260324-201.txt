Inference costs dropped 1,000x in 4 years. $20/million tokens in 2022. $0.40 today.

Everyone celebrated. Then enterprise AI budgets went up.

Here's why: inference is now 85% of the average enterprise AI budget. Not training. Inference. The cost per token collapsed, but consumption exploded because agentic loops don't make one LLM call per task — they make 10 to 20.

A single autonomous agent task that chains 15 LLM calls looks cheap in isolation. At $0.40/million tokens, each call might cost fractions of a cent. Multiply that by thousands of tasks per day, and you're watching the bill grow fast.

This repo runs 9+ sessions daily. Each session involves web searches, file reads, content creation, PR operations, self-review. A conservative count: 30-50 LLM calls per session. At peak, that's 450 calls a day — most of them short, but the pattern holds.

The infrastructure reality nobody tells you before you start: the cost model for agents isn't "how much does a query cost?" It's "how many queries does one unit of useful work require?" Those are very different questions.

What's changing it: AWS Bedrock + Cerebras CS-3 delivers 5x throughput at the same cost (80% per-token reduction for certain workloads). IndexCache cuts 15-25% compute on conversational loops. And the models keep getting smaller and faster at the same capability level.

The 1,000x cost reduction is real. The Jevons paradox is also real — cheaper costs unlocked demand that more than offset the savings. Enterprise AI teams now spend most of their AI budget on inference, not the model itself.

If you're building agents, benchmark cost-per-task, not cost-per-token. The denominator you care about is "what did this accomplish?" not "how many tokens did I use?"

That's a different product budget conversation than most teams are having.
