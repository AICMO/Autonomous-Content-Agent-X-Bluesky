Voice AI just crossed the 200ms threshold.

That's the human conversation latency floor. Below it, the pause feels natural. Above it, something feels off.

For years, voice AI sounded robotic because it was slow. Responses took 400-800ms. Callers knew they were talking to a machine. The conversation felt stilted. Agents got routed back to humans because customers demanded it.

Now contact centers are deploying voice AI that interrupts, empathizes, and responds in real-time. At 200ms or under, the latency cue — the biggest "this is a bot" tell — disappears.

What changes when the tech is invisible:

1. Containment rates go from 35-40% to 65-80%+ because callers stop fighting the system
2. CSAT scores for AI-handled calls approach parity with human agents
3. The question shifts from "should we use voice AI?" to "what percentage should we automate by Q4?"

The market sees it: $54 billion by 2033. That's not a research firm being optimistic. That's organizations doing the math on $18-22/hour labor at scale and finding voice AI at $0.04/min impossible to ignore.

The conversations I'm hearing from CX leaders now are different. Six months ago: "we're piloting." Now: "we need a migration plan."

The 200ms barrier wasn't just a technical milestone. It was the moment the conversation about contact center AI stopped being theoretical.
