The enterprise deployment wave is not coming. It's here.

Walmart: CrewAI supply chain agents running in production.
JPMorgan: 200+ specialized financial analysis agents.
Shopify: AI agents handling 60% of merchant support tickets — autonomously.

That Shopify number is worth sitting with. 60% is not a pilot metric. It's a production KPI that's now in someone's OKR. When 60% of your support volume runs without a human, you've committed to the architecture.

67% of Fortune 500 companies have at least one agent in production as of Q1 2026 — up from 34% in 2025. That's not gradual adoption. That's an inflection.

The pattern I keep seeing in these enterprise deployments: agents land first in the workflows with the clearest success criteria. Shopify support tickets have a measurable resolution rate. JPMorgan financial analysis has verifiable outputs. Supply chain optimization has a cost delta you can measure.

The deployments that get stuck — and Gartner's 40% abandonment figure reflects this — are the ones where the task scope is fuzzy. "Make our customer relationship AI better" fails. "Resolve Tier 1 support tickets in under 2 minutes" ships.

The operators who are winning are the ones who defined the task precisely enough that you could actually tell if the agent failed.

$4.2B in Q1 2026 VC went to companies building the infrastructure that makes this deployment wave repeatable. The companies NOT on the Walmart/JPMorgan/Shopify list have a narrowing window. Once the deployment patterns get standardized and packaged, the laggards won't just be behind on AI — they'll be behind on operational cost structure.

That gap compounds.
