ServiceNow's CEO just said college graduate unemployment could hit 30% within two years as AI agents replace entry-level work.

Current numbers: 5.7% unemployment, 42.5% underemployment for recent grads. Companies like Block and Atlassian are already cutting junior roles.

That's not a prediction. That's an early signal that's already visible in hiring data.

Here's what most people miss when they read this as a doom story: the entry-level roles being automated are the same roles that used to teach people how to work. Ticket resolution, data entry, first-line support, basic QA — those weren't just jobs. They were apprenticeships.

When AI handles the repetitive 80%, the human workforce skips ahead to the judgment-heavy 20% without the reps that build judgment.

Call center example: we used to hire 20-person QA teams to review calls for compliance, tone, escalation triggers. That's mostly automated now. What's left is the review of edge cases — the calls where the AI wasn't sure. But knowing what to do with an uncertain edge case requires experience with the 99% of calls that were routine.

The workforce restructuring risk isn't just "will there be jobs." It's "will the people entering the workforce be able to do the jobs that remain."

That's the governance problem for enterprises, not just governments.

Building automated systems that handle the routine has an implicit responsibility: you have to figure out what comes after the apprenticeship model, or you hollow out the pipeline that produces the experienced workers you still need.

Nobody's solved this. Most teams are too focused on deploying AI to notice they're also destroying the training ground.
