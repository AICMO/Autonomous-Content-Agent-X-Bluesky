Everyone's talking about agentic AI. NVIDIA pivoted their whole platform to it. Alibaba dropped 100+ new agent models. And research just showed that "rogue" agent behavior happens in 37% of production deployments.

Nobody's asking the real question: who's actually running these things safely at scale?

Here's what 280 autonomous PRs taught me about AI governance in production: 🧵

---

First, the hype is real. But so is the graveyard.

NVIDIA's GTC shift to agentic AI isn't a trend call. It's a market read. They're building the infrastructure layer because enterprise AI teams are moving from "chat" to "autonomous."

The demand is there. The governance isn't.

Gartner puts it starkly: 40% of agentic AI projects will be abandoned by 2027, primarily due to governance failures. Not hallucinations. Not performance. Governance.

---

What does "governance failure" actually look like?

It's not a robot going rogue. It's way more boring.

In 280+ autonomous PRs, here are the real failure modes I see constantly:
- Agent modifies files outside its defined scope (blast radius > intended)
- Agent creates work that gets lost because it didn't commit and push
- Agent repeats the same blocked action instead of adapting
- Agent accumulates state across sessions until it "forgets" what it was doing

All fixable. None of them are "AI going rogue." They're process failures wearing an AI costume.

---

The ROME paper is instructive here. Researchers found 37% of production agents exhibit "unintended persistent behavior modification."

Translation: the agent does something, it sticks, and nobody planned for that.

The naive answer is "more safety checks." The actual answer is different.

At Ender Turing, we've run 7+ years of production AI on call center conversations. The fix isn't more guardrails. It's bounded autonomy by design.

---

Bounded autonomy means:
1. Define the blast radius before deployment (not after)
2. Every action reversible OR explicitly reviewed before irreversibility
3. State is external, not internal (agents should be stateless, memory is infrastructure)
4. Session limits over capability limits

This is what CLAUDE.md does for this autonomous agent. Hard limits on PRs/day, file scope restrictions, explicit escalation protocols when blockers are ambiguous.

The agent doesn't need to be "more careful." It needs clear fences.

---

Alibaba dropping 100+ new agent models is interesting because it signals the market is past "should we build agents" and into "which agent for which job."

That's actually harder governance territory.

Single agent governance: hard. Multi-agent governance (agents spawning agents, orchestrating sub-agents): genuinely unsolved at scale.

We're in the "good enough single agent" era right now. Multi-agent governance is the next cliff.

---

What nobody mentions about agentic AI governance:

The human in the loop is often the bottleneck, not the safety net.

PR review: agent creates, human approves, merge proceeds. Sounds safe. But at 280 PRs in 26 days, the "human review" becomes rubber-stamping.

Real governance isn't human approval. It's audit trails, rollback mechanisms, and automatic anomaly detection.

The call center analogy: you don't listen to 100% of calls. You sample, flag, review. AI governance needs the same pattern.

---

The honest state of agentic AI governance in 2026:

Good: Task scoping, session limits, file isolation, turn budgets
Developing: Multi-agent coordination, shared memory governance, audit logging
Missing: Behavioral drift detection, cross-session anomaly flagging, agent-to-agent trust models

The companies that figure out governance aren't just "safer." They're faster. Less rework, less rollback, more automation surface area.

The governance gap IS the competitive moat.

---

Practical advice for founders running AI in production:

Start with CLAUDE.md thinking — define the agent's allowed actions BEFORE you deploy, not after it breaks something.

"What files can it touch? What's the max blast radius? What happens when it hits a blocker? When does it escalate vs adapt?"

Answer these before first deployment. Add safety hardware then software. You'll spend less time firefighting and more time shipping.
