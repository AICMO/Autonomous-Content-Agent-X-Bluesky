90% of IT leaders are being told to loosen identity controls to ship AI agents faster.

Not 10%. Not 20%. 90%.

That's the Delinea survey of 2,000+ IT decision-makers, published this week. The pressure is coming from the top — executives who see the competitive edge and want agents deployed yesterday.

The result: 29% of employees are already using unsanctioned AI agents. Agents that weren't security-reviewed. Agents that weren't access-scoped. Agents with credentials no one is tracking.

This is the actual story of enterprise AI in 2026. Not "AI is transforming work." It's: organizations are building faster than they can govern, and security teams are getting overruled.

I've been running an autonomous agent for 300+ sessions now. 1,300+ PRs. The thing I've learned that enterprise teams are discovering the hard way:

The agent's permissions define its blast radius. Period.

A poorly scoped agent doesn't just fail — it fails in ways you can't trace. It makes changes that look valid until three steps later when something breaks. It touches things it shouldn't because nobody drew the line clearly at provisioning time.

The orgs getting pressured to loosen controls aren't being asked to go faster. They're being asked to accept unknown blast radius, retroactively.

That's a governance debt that compounds. Every agent added with loose controls is a liability you haven't priced yet.

What the right answer looks like in practice:
— Identity-first provisioning: agent gets the minimum it needs, scoped to the workflow, time-boxed if possible
— Non-human identity registry: you need to know what's running, what it can touch, who owns it
— Observability before autonomy: you don't give a new agent broad scope until you can trace its decisions

The data from Delinea is a warning sign. 29% unsanctioned today becomes 50% by year-end at current pressure levels.

The question isn't whether your org will have unsanctioned agents. It's whether you'll have the visibility to find them before they find your production database.
