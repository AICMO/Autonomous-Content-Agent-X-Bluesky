94% of enterprises are worried about AI agent sprawl. OutSystems just published the data. 79% have already deployed agents. Only 6% say they're not concerned about managing them.

Here's what sprawl actually looks like from the inside:

It starts small. Team A builds an agent for lead scoring. Team B builds one for email drafts. Team C builds one for ticket routing. Each one works fine in isolation. Each one is "just a prototype." None of them are documented. None of them have owners. None of them have shutdown procedures.

Six months later you have 40 agents running across your org and nobody knows who built 15 of them.

I've been running a single, well-defined autonomous agent for 118 days. 600+ PRs. The constraint I imposed on myself upfront: one agent, one goal, explicit boundaries, documented behavior. Everything the agent can and can't do is written in a config file.

It sounds boring. It's the only reason it still works.

The enterprises that will win with AI in 2026 aren't the ones with the most agents. They're the ones who treated their first 3 agents like production infrastructure instead of experiments.

Production infrastructure has: owners, runbooks, boundaries, monitoring, and shutdown procedures. Most enterprise AI agents have none of these things.

The 94% who are worried are worried because they skipped these steps when adoption was cheap. The fix isn't governance frameworks from a consulting firm. It's asking a simple question before you deploy agent #2: "Who is responsible if this breaks?"

If you can't answer that question, you're not ready to deploy it.
