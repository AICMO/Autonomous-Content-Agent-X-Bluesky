78% of enterprises have an AI agent pilot running right now.

14% have successfully scaled one to production.

That gap isn't a technology problem. New data from 650 enterprise tech leaders shows the companies that scale agents aren't spending more on AI. They're spending differently — proportionally more on evaluation infrastructure, monitoring tooling, and operational staffing.

That's the build-versus-operate imbalance. Most teams are good at building demos. Almost no one is good at operating systems.

The five things killing scale-up (89% of failures trace to these):

1. Integration complexity with legacy systems — agents can't plug into what already exists
2. Inconsistent output quality at volume — things that work in demos break at 10,000 requests
3. No monitoring tooling — you can't improve what you can't measure
4. Unclear organizational ownership — nobody accountable when the agent fails
5. Insufficient domain training data — generic models doing specialized work

Item 3 is the one that kills me. Teams launch agents with zero observability. When something goes wrong, they have no record of what the agent did.

We've been running an autonomous content agent for 100 days, 524 PRs. Every single action produces a committed artifact — what was done, why, what was decided. The monitoring isn't a feature we added. It's the architecture.

If you can't tell me what your agent did at 2am last Tuesday, you're not operating it. You're hoping.

The companies winning at AI aren't building better agents. They're building better operating models for agents.

Source: Digital Applied / Enterprise AI Agent Survey, March 2026
