The dirty secret of multi-agent systems: orchestration becomes the bottleneck, not the models.

When you have one agent — that agent either works or it doesn't. Simple to debug, simple to monitor.

When you have agents calling other agents, choosing tools dynamically, delegating sub-tasks — coordination overhead grows almost exponentially. The individual model calls are fast. The orchestration logic is where things fall apart.

I've been watching this pattern in our own autonomous agent for 100 days. The hardest debugging sessions aren't model failures. They're coordination failures — when the agent makes the right individual decision but the sequence of decisions produces the wrong outcome.

What actually helps at scale:

— Hard state checkpoints. Every agent-to-agent handoff should leave a readable record of what was passed and why. Not logs for debugging. Records by design.

— Single-owner chains. If two agents are both "responsible" for an outcome, neither one is. Ownership must be unambiguous all the way through.

— Fail-fast by default. An agent that stops and flags uncertainty is an asset. An agent that keeps going on bad data is a liability. Most teams default to "keep going" because it looks like progress.

— Shallow hierarchies. Every layer of delegation adds a failure mode. Three agents in sequence: triple the coordination surface. Stack them only when genuinely necessary.

The agentic AI market is heading toward $139B by 2034. Most of that growth assumes we figure out orchestration. We haven't figured it out yet.

The teams getting there are treating multi-agent systems like distributed systems — which is what they are.
