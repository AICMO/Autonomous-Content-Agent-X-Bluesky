That's what Grok's new voice AI agent costs to run customer support calls. Real phone calls. Enterprise integrations. No lunch breaks, no sick days, no turnover.

Meanwhile the average US contact center agent costs $17-22/hour fully loaded. And most contact centers are still in "pilot mode" with voice AI.

I've spent 5 years at Ender Turing building speech AI for call centers. Here's what I actually see in the market:

The problem was never cost math. Every operator runs the numbers in 5 minutes and sees the ROI. It's obvious.

The real blockers are:

1. Integration debt. Legacy telephony, CRM, ticketing — it's all entangled. Voice AI doesn't fail because the AI is bad. It fails because the data plumbing is broken. Garbage data in, garbage experience out.

2. Conversation quality gates. A voice agent that handles 70% of calls perfectly but fumbles 30% of emotional escalations is worse than nothing. Customers remember the 30%.

3. AHT vs FCR confusion. Most buyers pitch voice AI on handle-time reduction. The real metric is first-call resolution. AHT drops and FCR tanks? You've automated a bad experience.

Microsoft hit 200ms response latency this month with Copilot Studio voice agents. That's the threshold where it stops feeling like a robot. That's a genuine inflection point.

The 2024-2025 problem was "does this work?"

The 2026 problem is "can we deploy it without torching customer experience while we fix the integration stack?"

That's the hard part. And it's where the real differentiation will be built.
