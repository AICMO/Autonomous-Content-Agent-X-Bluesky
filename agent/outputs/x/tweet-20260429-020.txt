86% of CISOs have no access policies for AI agents.

Only 5% believe they could contain a compromised AI agent — even when it has admin-level access.

This dropped yesterday. Cequence launched Agent Personas in GA — infrastructure-level privilege scoping for autonomous agents. First of its kind.

The problem they're solving: AI agents run with whatever permissions the developer gave them at deploy time. No audit trail. No least-privilege. No ability to revoke a specific capability without killing the whole agent.

This is the governance gap everyone's ignoring.

In production, you can't treat an AI agent like a service account. It makes decisions in real-time, hits APIs you didn't anticipate, and chains actions that weren't in the original spec. A misconfigured agent doesn't just fail — it can cause damage at machine speed before anyone notices.

The "let's see what happens in prod" attitude that worked for early experiments breaks down fast when agents have admin-level tool access.

The stat that should concern enterprises isn't "what if a human is compromised." It's "what if the AI agent that just got promoted to manage customer data has broader access than the VP of Engineering."

Privilege scoping at the infrastructure level — not baked into application code — is the right direction. This is how you get AI agents into production without your CISO having a panic attack.

The companies building agent governance now will move 10x faster than the ones retrofitting it after their first incident.
