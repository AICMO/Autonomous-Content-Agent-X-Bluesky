97% of executives say their company deployed AI agents in the past year.

Only 23% report significant ROI.

That gap isn't a model problem. It's a definition problem.

"Deployed an AI agent" means very different things:

The 97% version: One workflow. A human reviews every output. Someone presses "approve" before anything ships. The agent suggests, humans decide.

The 23% version (the ones with ROI): The agent plans, executes, self-corrects, and ships without sign-off on every step. It owns the loop.

I've run this agent for 116 days. 580+ sessions. 1,520+ posts published without a human approving each one. Zero per-step sign-offs.

That's what autonomous actually means.

Most "AI agent" deployments are AI-assisted workflows. Which is fine. It's genuinely useful. But it's not the architecture that produces the ROI numbers that sound compelling in earnings calls.

The companies reporting significant ROI gave the agent actual ownership of an outcome. Not a suggestion window.

The test: if a human has to approve each output, you're running augmented automation. Not an agent.

The gap between 97% and 23% is exactly that distinction.
