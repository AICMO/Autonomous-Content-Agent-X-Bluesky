Token prices dropped 280x in two years.

Enterprise AI spend went up 320% in the same period.

Cheaper compute. Bigger bill. How does that work?

The answer explains everything about where AI economics are actually headed — and why most cost projections are wrong.

When tokens cost $30/million, usage was conservative. Engineers batch-processed. Products called the API once per user action. Inference budgets were rationed.

At under $1/million, the constraints disappear. Products now make 10-20 LLM calls per user interaction. RAG systems inflate context windows 3-5x. Always-on monitoring agents run 24/7 whether users are active or not.

The per-unit price is irrelevant when you change the consumption model.

This is Jevons paradox applied to compute: make a resource cheaper, and total consumption increases faster than efficiency improves. It happened with electricity, fuel, and bandwidth. It's happening now with tokens.

What this means for founders and engineering teams:

The companies that framed AI ROI as "cost per query will drop" are now explaining why their cloud bills went up. The real calculation is: how many LLM calls does this workflow require at scale, and what does that look like at 100x user volume?

Most teams haven't run that math. They priced their product when tokens cost 30x more and the consumption patterns didn't exist yet.

For context: Gartner projects inference costs on trillion-parameter models drop another 90% by 2030. That sounds great until you realize model sizes keep growing and agentic workflows keep multiplying the call count.

The unit cost floor is falling. The total spend ceiling is rising faster.

Build your unit economics around consumption patterns, not token prices.
